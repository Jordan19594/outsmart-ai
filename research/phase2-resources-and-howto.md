## 1. AARP Fraud Watch Network

### Program Overview

The AARP Fraud Watch Network is the largest nonprofit anti-fraud initiative in the United States, operating as a free resource available to all consumers regardless of AARP membership. Launched initially as an extension of AARP's decades-long consumer protection mission, the network has grown into a comprehensive ecosystem that combines education, victim support, legislative advocacy, and real-time fraud tracking across all 53 AARP state offices.

The network's three strategic pillars are:

- **Stay Informed:** Delivering up-to-date intelligence on emerging scams via Watchdog Alerts, webinars, tele-town halls, and the award-winning *The Perfect Scam* podcast.
- **Find Support:** Providing trained fraud specialists through the toll-free AARP Fraud Helpline and online victim support groups for emotional recovery.
- **Have a Voice:** Advocating at federal, state, and local levels for stronger consumer protection laws and enforcement.

## 2. State Attorney General Elder Fraud Programs

### Overview

State attorneys general serve as the primary consumer protection authority at the state level and have become increasingly active in combating elder financial fraud. Many states have established dedicated elder fraud or elder exploitation units within the Attorney General's office, creating specialized investigative and prosecutorial capacity. The National Association of Attorneys General (NAAG) maintains an Elder Justice initiative that coordinates resources, training, and policy guidance across state AG offices and has endorsed the Fraud and Scam Reduction Act to strengthen enforcement tools.

### States with Dedicated Elder Fraud Units

As of early 2026, the following states maintain dedicated elder fraud or elder exploitation sections within the Attorney General's office:

- **California** -- Division of Medi-Cal Fraud and Elder Abuse (DMFEA), one of the largest state-level elder fraud operations in the country.
- **Pennsylvania** -- Launched a new Elder Exploitation Section in November 2025 under AG Dave Sunday, specifically focused on financial theft and fraud targeting older Pennsylvanians.
- **New York** -- Medicaid Fraud Control Unit with elder abuse prosecution capacity; also maintains a dedicated Elder Abuse Helpline.
- **Florida** -- Elder Abuse and Financial Exploitation unit within the AG's office; one of the most active given the state's large senior population.
- **Texas** -- Consumer Protection Division handles elder fraud complaints with dedicated staff.
- **Illinois** -- Senior Citizens and People with Disabilities Division.
- **Massachusetts** -- Elder Protection Unit within the AG's office.
- **Tennessee** -- Works through the District Attorneys General Conference, which coordinates elder abuse prosecution statewide. Tennessee seniors lost more than $32.5 million to financial scams in recent reporting periods.
- **Ohio** -- Elder Justice Unit within the AG's Bureau of Criminal Investigation.
- **Washington** -- Consumer Protection Division with elder fraud specialization.

### Notable State-Level Enforcement Actions (2024--2026)

- **DOJ 2025 Annual Report:** The Department of Justice reported that older adults lose billions of dollars annually to elder fraud, with federal and state authorities continuing to invest in capacity building for prosecutors and law enforcement. The DOJ's Transnational Elder Fraud Strike Force specifically targets cross-border scams, including "family emergency" (grandparent) scams conducted by overseas criminal networks.
- **Pennsylvania (November 2025):** AG Dave Sunday launched the Elder Exploitation Section, consolidating investigation and prosecution resources for elder financial crimes across the state.
- **California (Ongoing):** The DMFEA continues large-scale investigations into Medi-Cal fraud schemes that disproportionately affect seniors in institutional care.
- **FTC (December 2025):** The FTC's annual report documented that total fraud losses reported by older adults aged 60 and over skyrocketed from approximately $600 million in 2020 to over $2.4 billion by 2024, underscoring the growing need for state-level enforcement capacity.

### How Seniors Can File Complaints at the State Level

1. **Contact Your State Attorney General's Consumer Protection Division.** Most states accept complaints online, by phone, and by mail. Visit your state AG's website for the specific intake form.
2. **Call the National Elder Fraud Hotline:** **833-FRAUD-11 (833-372-8311)** -- Operated by the Office for Victims of Crime (OVC), this hotline connects callers with case managers who can help navigate reporting to the appropriate state authorities.
3. **File with Adult Protective Services (APS):** Each state maintains an APS office that investigates reports of elder abuse, including financial exploitation. The Eldercare Locator (1-800-677-1116) can connect you with your local APS.
4. **Report to Local Law Enforcement:** For ongoing or emergency situations, file a police report with your local law enforcement agency.

## 3. Area Agencies on Aging (AAA)

### How the AAA Network Operates Nationally

Area Agencies on Aging form the backbone of the federal Older Americans Act (OAA) service delivery system. Established by amendments to the OAA in 1973, there are now over 600 AAAs operating across the United States, covering every county and tribal area. AAAs are funded through a combination of federal (Administration for Community Living), state, and local sources. They serve as planning and service coordination bodies that connect older adults with a wide range of programs covering nutrition, transportation, legal assistance, caregiver support, benefits enrollment, and increasingly, fraud prevention and financial exploitation protection.

The AAA network operates under the oversight of 56 State Units on Aging (SUAs), which distribute federal funds and set statewide aging priorities. At the local level, AAAs contract with community service providers to deliver direct services to older adults, typically aged 60 and over.

### Services Related to Fraud Prevention

AAAs increasingly incorporate fraud prevention into their service portfolios:

1. **Community Education Programs:** Many AAAs conduct in-person and virtual workshops on scam awareness, safe banking practices, and digital literacy. For example, SeniorAge Area Agency on Aging in Missouri maintains dedicated staff who work to educate individuals on identifying and protecting themselves from scams and fraud.
2. **Benefits Enrollment and Financial Counseling:** AAA staff help seniors navigate Medicare, Medicaid, Social Security, and other benefits programs, which helps guard against benefits fraud and impersonation scams.
3. **Referral to Adult Protective Services:** AAAs serve as a key referral pathway for suspected financial exploitation, connecting seniors and their families with APS investigators.
4. **Legal Assistance Programs:** Under the OAA, AAAs fund legal services providers who can assist with consumer protection issues, including fraud recovery.
5. **Long-Term Care Ombudsman Programs:** AAAs house or coordinate with ombudsman programs that investigate complaints in nursing homes and assisted living facilities, where financial exploitation by staff or visitors can occur.
6. **Money Management Programs:** Some AAAs offer volunteer-based bill-paying and financial management assistance for seniors who need help managing finances, providing a safeguard against exploitation.

### How Seniors Find Their Local AAA

There are two primary methods:

1. **Eldercare Locator:** Call **1-800-677-1116** (Monday--Friday, 9 a.m.--8 p.m. ET) or visit **eldercare.acl.gov**. This is a free public service of the Administration for Community Living (ACL) that connects older adults and their families with local aging services, including their Area Agency on Aging.
2. **Online Search:** The Eldercare Locator website (eldercare.acl.gov) provides a searchable database where seniors can enter their ZIP code or city and state to find their local AAA and available services.

## 4. Phone Screening Tools and Apps

Robocalls and phone-based scams remain one of the most common vectors for elder financial fraud. AI-generated voice clones and caller ID spoofing have made these threats more convincing than ever. Fortunately, a growing arsenal of built-in phone features, third-party apps, and regulatory frameworks now exists to help seniors filter out fraudulent calls.

### iPhone: "Silence Unknown Callers"

**What It Does:** When enabled, this built-in iOS feature (available since iOS 13) automatically silences incoming calls from numbers not found in the user's Contacts, recent outgoing calls, or Siri Suggestions. Silenced calls go directly to voicemail and appear in the Recents list. Legitimate callers who leave a voicemail can be called back.

**Setup Steps:**

1. Open the **Settings** app on your iPhone.
2. Scroll down and tap **Phone**.
3. Scroll down to the "Call Silencing and Blocked Contacts" section.
4. Tap **Silence Unknown Callers**.
5. Toggle the switch **on** (green).

**Important Notes:**
- Emergency calls (911) will always ring through.
- Calls from numbers you have recently called will still ring through.
- This feature does not block calls; it silences them. Callers can still leave voicemail.
- Seniors who rely on calls from doctors' offices, pharmacies, or other services should be aware that first-time callers from new numbers will be silenced. Checking voicemail regularly is important.

### Google Call Screen (Pixel Phones and Select Android Devices)

**What It Does:** Google Call Screen uses the Google Assistant to answer incoming calls, ask the caller to state their name and reason for calling, and provide a real-time transcript on screen. The user can then decide whether to pick up, send a quick reply, or mark the call as spam. On Pixel phones, the feature can be set to automatically screen suspected spam calls without any user intervention.

**Setup Steps:**

1. Open the **Phone** app on your Pixel or compatible Android device.
2. Tap the **three-dot menu** (top right).
3. Select **Settings**.
4. Tap **Call Screen** (or "Spam and Call Screen" on some models).
5. Toggle **Automatically screen calls** to on.
6. Choose your screening level: "Maximum protection" screens all calls from unknown numbers automatically.

**Important Notes:**
- Currently available on Google Pixel phones in the United States. Some features are available on select Samsung and other Android devices through the Google Phone app.
- The caller hears a Google Assistant greeting and is asked to identify themselves; the user sees a live transcript.
- Users can tap "Pick up" at any time to join the call.

### FCC Initiatives and STIR/SHAKEN Caller ID Authentication

**What Is STIR/SHAKEN?** STIR (Secure Telephone Identity Revisited) and SHAKEN (Signature-based Handling of Asserted information using toKENs) form a framework of interconnected standards that digitally validate the handoff of phone calls across networks. When a call passes through the system, the originating carrier attaches a digital certificate verifying that the calling number has not been spoofed. The terminating carrier can then verify this certificate and display a trust indicator to the consumer.

**Current Status (2025--2026):**

- The FCC mandated STIR/SHAKEN implementation for major carriers beginning June 2021, with smaller carriers given extended deadlines.
- A December 2025 Federal Register notice ("Advanced Methods to Target and Eliminate Robocalls") proposed strengthening the framework and addressing gaps in legacy TDM (non-IP) networks where STIR/SHAKEN cannot function.
- In early 2026, states began pushing their own caller ID verification legislation to complement the federal framework, with several states mandating STIR/SHAKEN compliance for intrastate calls.
- The FCC is exploring "Call Branding" requirements (October 2025 Fact Sheet) that would allow verified callers to display their business name and logo on the recipient's phone screen, further helping consumers distinguish legitimate calls from spoofed ones.
- Industry groups, including credit unions and banking associations, have urged the FCC to set firm deadlines for migrating all carriers off legacy TDM systems to ensure universal STIR/SHAKEN coverage.

**Limitations:** STIR/SHAKEN only works on IP-based networks. Calls originating on older copper-wire (TDM) networks or from international gateways may bypass the system entirely, which remains a significant gap exploited by overseas scam operations.

### National Do Not Call Registry

**How to Register:**

1. **By Phone:** Call **1-888-382-1222** from the phone number you wish to register.
2. **Online:** Visit **donotcall.gov** and enter your phone number and email address.
3. **Confirmation:** You must click a confirmation link sent to your email (for online registration).

**Key Facts:**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|

| **Effective** | 31 days after registration |
| **Duration** | Registration does not expire (since 2008 rule change) |

**Limitations:**
- Does not block all unwanted calls. Exemptions exist for political calls, charitable solicitations, survey calls, and debt collection calls.
- Does not stop illegal robocallers who disregard the law. Most scam operations operate illegally and ignore DNC status entirely.
- The FTC's 2025 Data Book reported that the Registry remains a useful tool for reducing calls from legitimate telemarketers, but it provides limited protection against criminal fraud operations.
- Seniors should combine DNC registration with call-blocking technology for maximum protection.

## 5. Passkey Setup Guides

Passkeys represent a fundamental shift in authentication technology, replacing passwords with cryptographic key pairs secured by biometrics (fingerprint, face scan) or device screen lock. For seniors, passkeys offer a significant security advantage: there is no password to remember, no password to be phished, and no password to be stolen in a data breach. Major technology companies and an increasing number of banks now support passkeys as of early 2026.

### What Is a Passkey?

A passkey is a digital credential stored on your device (phone, tablet, or computer) that lets you sign in to websites and apps using your fingerprint, face scan, or screen lock instead of a password. Passkeys are:

- **Phishing-resistant:** They cannot be typed into a fake website because they are cryptographically bound to the legitimate site.
- **Unique per site:** Each passkey works only for the specific service it was created for.
- **Synced securely:** On Apple devices, passkeys sync through iCloud Keychain. On Android, they sync through Google Password Manager.

### Step-by-Step: iPhone and iPad (iOS 16+)

**Prerequisites:** iPhone or iPad running iOS 16 or later; iCloud Keychain enabled; Face ID, Touch ID, or a passcode set up.

1. **Ensure iCloud Keychain is on:**
   - Open **Settings** > tap your name at the top > **iCloud** > **Passwords and Keychain** > toggle **Sync this iPhone** on.

2. **Create a passkey on a supported website or app:**
   - Navigate to the website or app that supports passkeys (e.g., Google, Microsoft, a supported bank).
   - Go to the site's **Security Settings** or **Sign-in options**.
   - Look for "Passkey," "Create a Passkey," or "Sign in with a passkey."
   - Tap **Create Passkey** (or similar button).
   - Your iPhone will prompt you to authenticate with **Face ID**, **Touch ID**, or your **passcode**.
   - The passkey is created and saved to iCloud Keychain automatically.

3. **Sign in with your passkey:**
   - On your next visit, tap the sign-in field.
   - Your iPhone will suggest the saved passkey in an autofill prompt.
   - Authenticate with Face ID, Touch ID, or your passcode.
   - You are signed in -- no password needed.

### Step-by-Step: Android

**Prerequisites:** Android 9 or later (Android 14+ recommended for best experience); Google account signed in; screen lock set up (fingerprint, face unlock, or PIN).

1. **Ensure Google Password Manager sync is on:**
   - Open **Settings** > **Google** > **Autofill** > **Google Password Manager** > ensure sync is enabled.

2. **Create a passkey (example using Google account):**
   - Open your browser and go to **myaccount.google.com**.
   - Tap **Security** > **How you sign in to Google** > **Passkeys and security keys**.
   - Tap **Create a passkey**.
   - Your device will prompt you to authenticate with your **fingerprint**, **face unlock**, or **screen lock PIN**.
   - The passkey is saved to Google Password Manager and syncs across your Android devices.

3. **Sign in with your passkey:**
   - Visit the website or app.
   - Tap the sign-in field; your device will suggest the passkey.
   - Authenticate with your biometric or screen lock.

### Step-by-Step: Gmail (Google Account)

1. Go to **myaccount.google.com** in your browser.
2. Tap **Security** in the left menu.
3. Under "How you sign in to Google," tap **Passkeys and security keys**.
4. Tap **Create a passkey**.
5. Follow the prompt to authenticate with your device biometric or screen lock.
6. Your passkey is saved. When you sign in to Gmail or any Google service, you will be prompted to use your passkey instead of a password.

### Step-by-Step: Outlook (Microsoft Account)

1. Go to **account.microsoft.com** and sign in.
2. Navigate to **Security** > **Advanced security options**.
3. Under "Additional security," find **Passkey** and tap **Add a new way to sign in**.
4. Select **Face, fingerprint, PIN, or security key**.
5. Follow the prompts to create and save a passkey on your device.
6. On your next sign-in to Outlook or any Microsoft service, select the passkey option.

### Common Senior Concerns and How to Address Them

| Concern | Response |
|---------|----------|
| "What if I lose my phone?" | Passkeys sync to your cloud account (iCloud or Google). If you get a new phone and sign in with the same Apple ID or Google account, your passkeys are restored automatically. |
| "What if my fingerprint doesn't work?" | All devices offer fallback options: you can use your face scan, PIN, or device passcode instead of a fingerprint. |
| "Is this less secure than a password?" | Passkeys are significantly more secure than passwords. They cannot be phished, guessed, or stolen in a data breach. |
| "Do I need to remember anything?" | No. Your device and biometric (fingerprint or face) replace the need to remember a password. |
| "What if the website doesn't support passkeys?" | Keep your existing password for sites that do not yet support passkeys. A password manager (see Section 6) can help manage those remaining passwords. |
| "Can my family help me set this up?" | Yes. A trusted family member can walk you through the setup process. The passkey itself is secured by your biometric, so no one else can use it without your fingerprint or face. |

## 7. Community Digital Literacy Programs

Digital literacy is foundational to fraud prevention. Seniors who understand how technology works, how to evaluate messages and websites critically, and how to configure privacy and security settings are far less likely to fall victim to scams. Several national programs and platforms provide free or low-cost digital skills training specifically designed for older adults.

### AARP TEK (Technology Education and Knowledge)

AARP TEK workshops are free, in-person and virtual sessions designed to help older adults build confidence with technology. Topics include:

- Smartphone and tablet basics
- Using apps safely
- Online shopping security
- Social media privacy
- Recognizing scams and phishing

**How to Access:** AARP TEK events are listed on local AARP chapter pages and through the AARP Events portal. Check aarp.org/events for upcoming sessions in your area.

### Senior Planet (from AARP)

Senior Planet, operated by Older Adults Technology Services (OATS) in partnership with AARP, is the premier national technology education program for older adults. It offers:

- **Live Online Classes:** Free Zoom-based classes taught by expert trainers. Weekly offerings cover fitness, wellness, finance, technology fundamentals, and cybersecurity. Available at seniorplanet.org/classes.
- **In-Person Technology Centers:** Located in select cities (New York City, Colorado, Florida, Maryland, and expanding), these centers offer hands-on instruction and community.
- **Digital Skills Ready@50+:** A targeted program focused on digital essentials for job seekers, career changers, and entrepreneurs over 50.
- **Hotline:** **888-713-3495** (Monday--Friday, 9 a.m.--8 p.m. ET; Saturday, 9 a.m.--2 p.m. ET).

**Scale:** OATS/Senior Planet offers hundreds of free virtual classes each week, making it one of the largest providers of senior digital literacy education in the country.

### Library-Based Digital Literacy Programs

Public libraries are among the most trusted and accessible institutions for senior digital literacy training. The Public Library Association (PLA), a division of the American Library Association (ALA), runs a national Digital Literacy initiative that supports libraries in delivering technology training:

- **DigitalLearn.org:** A free online resource hub created by PLA offering self-paced tutorials on topics including internet basics, email, online safety, and using mobile devices.
- **Library Hotspot Lending:** Many libraries lend Wi-Fi hotspots and laptops to seniors who lack home internet access.
- **In-Person Tech Help:** Libraries in urban and suburban areas frequently offer drop-in tech help sessions, one-on-one digital coaching, and group classes.
- **PLA Grants:** Participating libraries receive funding ($4,000--$10,000) and resource support to close the digital divide in their communities.

**How to Find Programs:** Contact your local public library or search for programs at digitallearn.org.

### GetSetUp

GetSetUp is an online platform and community specifically designed for older adults. It provides:

- **Live Interactive Classes:** Taught by peers (older adults who are trained instructors), covering technology, health, finance, and creative skills.
- **Small Class Sizes:** Typically 10--15 participants, allowing personalized attention.
- **Community Features:** Social groups, discussion forums, and events that combine learning with social connection.
- **Accessibility:** Large text, simple navigation, and patient instructors who understand older adult learning styles.

**Website:** getsetup.io (many classes are free; some premium offerings available).

### State and Federal Funding for Senior Digital Literacy

- **Older Americans Act (OAA) Title III-B:** Provides supportive services funding that AAAs can allocate toward technology training and digital literacy programs.
- **Institute of Museum and Library Services (IMLS):** Federal grants support library-based digital literacy programs serving older adults.
- **FCC Affordable Connectivity Program (ACP):** While the ACP's federal funding was exhausted in 2024, some states have created successor programs providing broadband subsidies for low-income seniors.
- **State-Level Initiatives:** States including California, New York, Florida, and Texas have allocated state funds for senior digital literacy. For example, Florida appropriated $854,461 for OATS/Senior Planet center operations in Miami for FY2025-26.
- **Digital Equity Act (DEA):** Passed as part of the 2021 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, the DEA provides $2.75 billion in grants to states, territories, and tribal entities to promote digital equity, with older adults designated as a covered population. State Digital Equity Plans are being implemented during 2025--2026.

## 8. Social Media Privacy Settings Walkthroughs

Social media profiles are a goldmine for scammers conducting reconnaissance before launching targeted fraud attacks. A publicly visible profile can reveal a senior's full name, birthday, hometown, family members' names, travel plans, and daily routines -- all of which can be used to craft convincing phishing messages, romance scams, or grandparent scams. Locking down privacy settings is a critical defensive step.

### Facebook Privacy Settings: Step-by-Step

Facebook is the most widely used social media platform among older adults. The following steps lock down the most important privacy controls.

#### A. Control Who Can See Your Posts

1. Open the **Facebook app** or go to **facebook.com**.
2. Tap your **profile picture** (bottom right on mobile, or top right on desktop).
3. Go to **Settings & Privacy** > **Settings**.
4. Tap **Privacy** (under "Audience and Visibility" on mobile; or "Privacy Center" on desktop).
5. Under **"Who can see your future posts?"** -- Change to **Friends** (not "Public").
6. Under **"Limit Past Posts"** -- Tap this option to change all previous public posts to Friends-only in one step. Confirm when prompted.

#### B. Control Who Can Send You Friend Requests

1. In **Settings** > **Privacy**, find **"Who can send you friend requests?"**
2. Change to **Friends of Friends** (this prevents strangers with no mutual connections from sending requests).

#### C. Control Who Can Look You Up

1. In **Settings** > **Privacy**, find the section on "How People Find and Contact You."
2. **"Who can look you up using the email address you provided?"** -- Set to **Friends** or **Only me**.
3. **"Who can look you up using the phone number you provided?"** -- Set to **Friends** or **Only me**.
4. **"Do you want search engines outside of Facebook to link to your profile?"** -- Toggle **Off**.

#### D. Review Your Profile's Public Visibility

1. Go to your **Profile** page.
2. Tap the **three dots** (...) near your profile picture.
3. Select **"View As"** (or "View as Public" on desktop).
4. This shows you exactly what a stranger would see on your profile. Review and remove or hide any sensitive information (birthday, phone number, address, workplace).

#### E. Review Tagged Photos and Posts

1. In **Settings** > **Profile and Tagging**:
   - **"Who can post on your profile?"** -- Set to **Friends** or **Only me**.
   - **"Review tags people add to your posts before the tags appear on Facebook?"** -- Toggle **On**.
   - **"Review posts you're tagged in before the post appears on your profile?"** -- Toggle **On**.

### Instagram Privacy Settings: Step-by-Step

#### A. Switch to a Private Account

1. Open the **Instagram app**.
2. Tap your **profile icon** (bottom right).
3. Tap the **three lines** (top right) > **Settings and Privacy**.
4. Under **"Who can see your content"**, tap **Account Privacy**.
5. Toggle **Private Account** to **On**.
6. When private, only approved followers can see your posts, stories, and reels.

#### B. Control Who Can Message You

1. In **Settings and Privacy** > **Messages and story replies**:
   - Under **"Message controls"**, set to receive message requests only from followers, or turn off message requests from non-followers entirely.

#### C. Control Who Can Tag You

1. In **Settings and Privacy** > **Tags and mentions**:
   - **"Allow tags from"** -- Set to **People you follow** or **No one**.
   - **"Allow mentions from"** -- Set to **People you follow** or **No one**.

#### D. Hide Your Activity Status

1. In **Settings and Privacy** > **Activity status**:
   - Toggle **Show activity status** to **Off**. This prevents others from seeing when you were last active.

### How to Audit What Is Publicly Visible

Performing a privacy audit of your social media presence is straightforward:

1. **Log out of your account** (or use a different browser/incognito mode).
2. **Search for your name** on Google. Note which social media profiles appear and what information is visible in the previews.
3. **Visit your Facebook profile** while logged out (or use the "View As" feature described above). Note any publicly visible photos, posts, or personal details.
4. **Search for yourself on Instagram** while logged out. If your account is private, only your profile picture and bio should be visible.
5. **Check data broker sites.** Services like DeleteMe, Optery, or manual opt-outs from sites like Spokeo, WhitePages, and BeenVerified can remove personal information that scammers aggregate from public records and social media.

**Recommended Audit Schedule:** Perform a privacy audit at least once every six months, or whenever you update your profile information.
