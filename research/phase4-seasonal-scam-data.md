# Phase 4: Seasonal Scam Intelligence Report
## Protecting U.S. Seniors from AI-Enabled Financial Fraud — Temporal Analysis

*Research compiled: February 2026*
*Sources: FTC, FBI IC3, AARP, DOJ, CRS, McAfee, NPR, CNBC*

---

## Executive Summary

The temporal dimension of financial fraud targeting older Americans represents one of the most actionable intelligence vectors available to consumer protection advocates. Scam operators calibrate their campaigns to exploit seasonal vulnerabilities — tax anxiety in spring, healthcare confusion in fall, emotional isolation during holidays, and crisis-driven urgency after natural disasters. The integration of artificial intelligence into these seasonal playbooks has dramatically amplified both the scale and persuasiveness of attacks.

The Federal Trade Commission's December 2025 annual report to Congress documented that reported fraud losses among adults aged 60 and older reached $2.4 billion in 2024, a 300% increase from $600 million in 2020. The FTC estimates actual losses, accounting for chronic underreporting, may reach $81.5 billion. The FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) recorded $4.885 billion in losses from 147,127 elder fraud complaints in 2024, with investment fraud and cryptocurrency scams driving the largest share. Losses of $100,000 or more per incident now account for 68% of total reported elder fraud dollars — a pattern that underscores the devastating, life-altering nature of these crimes.

This report maps the annual fraud cycle month by month, identifies AI-specific enhancements to each seasonal scam category, and provides a 12-month content planning framework for recurring newsletter deployment.

## 1. Tax Season Scams (January–April)

### Seasonal Trigger Profile

Tax filing season creates a concentrated window of anxiety, urgency, and government interaction that scammers exploit with precision. The IRS processing cycle — from W-2 distribution in late January through the April filing deadline — generates a four-month period during which Americans expect contact from tax authorities and financial institutions, lowering their defenses against impersonation attacks.

### Scale and Impact

According to a February 2025 McAfee survey of 3,000 U.S. adults, nearly one in four Americans report that they or someone they know has been a victim of a tax scam. The demographic breakdown reveals targeted exploitation:

| Age Group | Primary Attack Vector | Notable Statistic |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 18–24 | Identity harvesting (birth dates, bank info) | Highest volume of scam encounters |
| 35–54 | SSN/Tax ID theft, fraudulent back-tax demands | Over one-third received fake back-tax payment demands |
| 65–74 | Payment scams, fake refund harvesting | 45% reported fake tax refund scams requesting banking details; 37% received demands for immediate back-tax payments |

The FTC reported $12.5 billion in total consumer fraud losses in 2024, with a 25% increase over the prior year. Tax-season scams represent a substantial seasonal concentration within that figure. The average IRS identity theft case takes 506 days to resolve, compounding the financial harm with prolonged administrative burden.

### AI Enhancement Vectors

The 2025–2026 tax season has seen a qualitative shift in scam sophistication driven by AI:

- **Deepfake IRS voice calls**: AI-generated voice synthesis now produces calls that replicate the cadence, tone, and authority of government officials. Scammers use publicly available audio samples to clone specific voices or generate generic "authoritative government agent" personas that are nearly indistinguishable from legitimate callers.
- **AI-generated phishing correspondence**: Fraudulent IRS notices, emails, and SMS messages now exhibit near-perfect grammar, formatting, and branding. AI tools generate IRS letterhead reproductions, complete with correct form numbers, that surpass the quality of previous template-based approaches.
- **Synthetic tax preparation websites**: AI enables rapid generation of convincing replicas of TurboTax, H&R Block, and IRS.gov interfaces. These sites harvest credentials and financial data while appearing functionally identical to legitimate platforms.
- **Smishing at scale**: AI-powered text message campaigns distribute fake refund notifications containing malicious links. Over one-third of McAfee survey respondents reported receiving such messages.

### IRS Official Communication Policies

The IRS has established clear communication boundaries that serve as definitive scam indicators:

1. The IRS will **never** initiate contact via phone, email, text, or social media to request personal or financial information
2. The IRS will **never** demand immediate payment via wire transfer, gift card, or cryptocurrency
3. The IRS will **never** threaten arrest, deportation, or license revocation for unpaid taxes
4. Initial IRS contact is **always** by postal mail
5. Taxpayers can verify any purported IRS communication by calling 1-800-829-1040

### Case Study: Multi-State IRS Back-Tax Phone Scam

The IRS back-tax phone scam has generated millions in losses, with scammers calling victims claiming they owe back taxes and face immediate arrest. In AI-enhanced variants, the caller's voice is synthesized to sound like a specific regional IRS office, and caller ID is spoofed to display legitimate IRS numbers. Victims are directed to purchase gift cards or wire funds to avoid prosecution.

## 2. Medicare Open Enrollment Scams (October–December)

### Seasonal Trigger Profile

Medicare Open Enrollment (October 15 – December 7) creates one of the most exploitable seasonal windows in the fraud calendar. The convergence of genuine plan changes, legitimate insurer outreach, and the complexity of Medicare itself produces an environment where fraudulent contact is difficult to distinguish from authentic communication. October also coincides with Cybersecurity Awareness Month, an irony not lost on security professionals.

### Scale and Impact

During the 2024 Open Enrollment period, the FTC reported over 45,000 Medicare-related fraud complaints, with losses exceeding $100 million. The 2025 enrollment period saw further escalation as scammers integrated AI tools into their operations.

The FTC's December 2025 report to Congress noted that older adults are 36% more likely to report losses on romance scams and disproportionately affected by government impersonation scams — a category that encompasses Medicare fraud.

### Seven Primary Attack Vectors

Based on FTC enforcement data and field reporting from the All Seniors Foundation and Senior Medicare Patrol programs, the following scam types dominate the enrollment period:

1. **Fake Medicare representative phone calls** — Caller ID spoofed to display 1-800-MEDICARE; scripts reference real plan changes to establish credibility; requests for Medicare numbers, SSNs, or banking information
2. **Fraudulent plan comparison websites** — AI-generated sites mimicking Medicare.gov or legitimate insurer portals; harvest personal data through functional-appearing enrollment forms
3. **Door-to-door Medicare sales scams** — Prohibited by federal regulation yet persistent; impersonators claim to conduct mandatory surveys or deliver new Medicare cards
4. **"New Medicare card" phishing** — Recycled annually with updated pretexts; now delivered via email, SMS, and social media with AI-generated graphics
5. **Genetic testing and medical equipment fraud** — Offers for "free" DNA cancer screenings at community events to harvest Medicare numbers for billing fraud
6. **Social media Medicare scams** — AI-generated deepfake videos of celebrities endorsing Medicare plans; infiltration of senior-focused Facebook groups
7. **Phantom plan cancellation threats** — False claims that current coverage is being discontinued to panic victims into providing information

### AI Enhancement Vectors

- **Voice-cloned Medicare representatives**: AI voice synthesis creates convincing "Medicare agent" personas that maintain consistent tone across extended calls, including fake transfers between "departments"
- **AI-generated mailers and emails**: Physical mail pieces and digital communications now replicate CMS branding with high fidelity, including correct Medicare Beneficiary Identifier formats
- **Deepfake endorsement videos**: AI-generated video content featuring synthetic versions of trusted public figures endorsing specific Medicare plans appears in social media advertising

### Verification Protocol

- Medicare will **never** call uninvited to sell plans or request personal information
- Medicare will **never** visit homes without a scheduled, beneficiary-initiated appointment
- All Medicare questions should be directed to **1-800-MEDICARE (1-800-633-4227)**
- Plan comparisons should only be conducted at **Medicare.gov**
- Free, unbiased counseling is available through **State Health Insurance Assistance Programs (SHIPs)**

## 3. Holiday Season Scams (November–December)

### Seasonal Trigger Profile

The November–December holiday period concentrates multiple fraud accelerants: increased online shopping, charitable giving impulses, gift card purchasing, and package delivery volume. Each of these creates distinct attack surfaces that scammers exploit simultaneously, generating a compounding effect that makes the holiday season the highest-volume period for consumer fraud complaints.

### Scale and Impact

The Senior List reported that seniors lost $1.5 billion to fraud in the first half of 2025 alone, with gift card schemes accounting for 6% and fake charities 2% of reported losses. AARP research found that 72% of consumers planned to purchase gift cards during the 2025 holiday season, creating an expansive target surface for gift card fraud.

Travel fraud surged 18% during summer 2024, and the holiday shopping season saw comparable spikes in online retail fraud. The FTC's total 2024 fraud loss figure of $12.5 billion includes significant holiday-season concentration.

### Primary Attack Categories

**Fake Charity Solicitations**
- AI-generated charity websites complete with synthetic spokesperson videos, fabricated 501(c)(3) documentation, and convincing impact narratives
- Exploitation of year-end tax-deduction motivations
- Impersonation of established charities with minor name variations (e.g., "American Red Cross Foundation" vs. the legitimate American Red Cross)

**Online Shopping Fraud**
- AI-generated fake storefronts offering luxury goods at implausible discounts
- Social media advertising linking to convincing but fraudulent e-commerce sites
- "Deal-too-good-to-be-true" pricing designed to override rational evaluation

**Gift Card Scams**
- Physical tampering with gift cards at retail locations (draining balances after recording card numbers)
- Scammers directing victims to purchase gift cards as "payment" for fictitious debts or fees
- Gift card activation fraud through compromised point-of-sale systems

**Package Delivery Smishing**
- Surge in fake USPS, UPS, and FedEx delivery notification texts during peak shipping season
- AI-generated tracking pages that harvest personal information
- "Failed delivery" notifications requiring "redelivery fees"

### AI-Specific Holiday Threats

- AI-generated product review ecosystems that create false credibility for fraudulent storefronts
- Synthetic customer service chatbots on scam websites that maintain convincing interactions
- AI-powered price-matching scam engines that generate custom "deals" based on browsing history

## 4. Romance Scam Cycles (Valentine's Day, Holidays, Post-Bereavement)

### Seasonal Trigger Profile

Romance scams exhibit distinct seasonal spikes correlated with periods of heightened emotional vulnerability. The primary peaks occur around Valentine's Day (mid-January through February), the Thanksgiving-Christmas-New Year holiday cluster (November–January), and following major life events such as bereavement — which, while not calendar-bound, correlates with increased online social engagement by seniors.

### Scale and Impact

The FTC estimated $2.4 billion was lost to romance scams in 2024, with median per-person losses of $2,000 — the highest median loss for any form of impersonation fraud. The FBI IC3's 2024 report documented that investment fraud, much of which originates through romance-based social engineering (pig butchering), accounted for over $6.5 billion in total losses, with cryptocurrency investment scams alone reaching $6.5 billion.

Older adults are disproportionately affected. The FTC's December 2025 report noted that adults aged 60+ are 36% more likely to report romance scam losses compared to younger demographics. The National Council on Aging identifies pig butchering as a hybrid scheme that specifically targets older adults by combining romance fraud with investment fraud.

### Romance-to-Investment Pipeline (Pig Butchering)

The "pig butchering" (sha zhu pan) model represents the most financially devastating evolution of romance scams:

1. **Contact phase**: Initial outreach via social media, dating apps, or "wrong number" text messages
2. **Grooming phase**: Weeks to months of relationship building, often using AI-generated conversation to maintain consistent, emotionally responsive communication
3. **Introduction phase**: The scammer introduces a "great investment opportunity," typically involving cryptocurrency
4. **Fattening phase**: Small initial "investments" show fabricated returns on fake trading platforms, building confidence
5. **Slaughter phase**: Victim invests substantial savings; platform freezes funds or demands "tax" payments to release returns; funds are irrecoverable

### AI Enhancement Vectors

- **AI emotionally intelligent chatbots**: Large language models now power romance scam conversations, enabling scammers to maintain dozens of simultaneous "relationships" with consistent emotional tone, memory of previous conversations, and adaptive response to victim emotional states
- **Deepfake video calls**: AI-generated real-time video enables scammers to conduct "face-to-face" calls using synthetic faces, overcoming a traditional verification method
- **AI-generated social media profiles**: Complete synthetic identities with years of backdated posts, AI-generated photos, and fabricated social networks

### Senior-Specific Vulnerability Factors

- Post-retirement social isolation increases susceptibility to relationship-based manipulation
- Bereavement creates acute emotional vulnerability, with scammers monitoring obituaries and memorial pages
- Generational trust in presented identity (less skepticism about digital identity verification)
- Larger accumulated savings create higher-value targets

### Case Study: AI-Powered Pig Butchering Network

The FBI and IC3 have documented networks operating from Southeast Asian compounds where workers — often trafficking victims themselves — use AI chatbot tools to manage hundreds of simultaneous romance scam conversations. The Congressional Research Service reported that of 859,532 IC3 complaints in 2024, 147,127 involved elder fraud, with cryptocurrency investment scams (often originating from romance-based social engineering) generating $4.8 billion in losses among victims over 60.

## 5. Natural Disaster Scams (Event-Driven)

### Seasonal Trigger Profile

Natural disaster scams are event-driven rather than calendar-fixed, but follow predictable seasonal patterns: Atlantic hurricane season (June–November), western wildfire season (summer–fall), and severe weather events (spring tornadoes, winter storms). The critical fraud window opens within 24–48 hours of a declared disaster and remains active for months during recovery.

### Recent Event-Specific Fraud

**Hurricane Helene and Milton (October 2024)**
The FTC issued specific consumer alerts regarding recovery scams following Hurricane Helene, warning of FEMA impersonators charging "application fees" and fraudulent contractors soliciting upfront payments. Federal prosecutors in the Western District of North Carolina indicted Peggy Lee Cantrell of Marion, NC, for defrauding FEMA following Tropical Storm Helene, filing fraudulent FEMA applications for disaster relief funds.

**Los Angeles Wildfires (January–February 2025)**
Three individuals were charged with FEMA fraud during LA wildfire recovery, with one defendant accused of stealing $12,314.52 from FEMA through fraudulent applications misrepresenting disaster damage. Social media platforms were flooded with conspiracy theories and fraudulent fundraising appeals.

### Primary Attack Vectors

1. **FEMA impersonation scams** — Callers claim to be FEMA agents requiring fees to process disaster assistance applications (FEMA never charges application fees)
2. **Fraudulent GoFundMe and charity pages** — AI-generated disaster relief websites with synthetic victim testimonials and fabricated damage photos
3. **Contractor fraud** — Fake repair companies demanding upfront payment for work never performed
4. **Insurance claim scams** — Impersonation of insurance adjusters seeking policy and financial information
5. **Government grant scams** — False offers of disaster relief grants requiring "processing fees"

### AI Enhancement Vectors

- AI-generated images of disaster damage for use in fraudulent fundraising campaigns
- Synthetic voice calls impersonating FEMA representatives or local emergency management officials
- AI-powered fake charity websites that generate convincing organizational histories, financial disclosures, and impact reports
- Deepfake videos of public officials or disaster victims soliciting donations

### Verification Resources

- FEMA Disaster Fraud Hotline: **866-720-5721**
- Verify charities at **give.org** (BBB Wise Giving Alliance) or **charitynavigator.org**
- FEMA never charges application fees or requests banking information via phone
- Legitimate disaster assistance is available at **DisasterAssistance.gov**

## 6. Back-to-School / Grandparent Targeting (August–September)

### Seasonal Trigger Profile

The August–September back-to-school and college move-in period generates a documented spike in grandparent scams. The logical narrative — "your grandchild was arrested at school" or "your grandchild was in a car accident near campus" — becomes maximally plausible when grandchildren are actually transitioning to new educational environments.

### Scale and Impact

In March 2025, federal prosecutors announced that 25 Canadian nationals were charged with defrauding hundreds of American seniors out of more than $21 million through a grandparent scam operation. The scheme, operating from call centers in the Montreal area from summer 2021 through June 2024, targeted elderly victims in 46 states.

The Yahoo News / AI fraud reporting ecosystem documented that AI voice cloning scams targeting seniors generated approximately $5 billion in losses in 2024, with grandparent scams representing a significant portion. A single AI voice clone requires as little as 30 seconds of audio — readily available from social media posts, voicemail greetings, or video content.

### Operational Mechanics

The indicted Canadian operation reveals the industrial scale of modern grandparent scams:

1. **Target acquisition**: Spreadsheets containing elderly Americans' personal information including age and household income
2. **Initial contact**: Spoofed phone calls impersonating a grandchild claiming a car accident and arrest
3. **Isolation**: False claim of a "gag order" preventing the victim from contacting other family members
4. **Escalation**: Transfer to a second scammer posing as an attorney representing the grandchild
5. **Collection**: In-person money pickup by a third co-conspirator posing as a bail bondsman, or mailing of cash
6. **Laundering**: Funds transmitted to Canada, often converted to cryptocurrency
7. **Repeat targeting**: Victims who paid were called again with claims of increased bail — internally classified as "whales"

### AI Voice Cloning Enhancement

NBC Philadelphia reported on an 86-year-old South Philadelphia woman whose family says scammers used AI to impersonate her grandchild's voice, stealing thousands of dollars. The American Bar Association's September 2025 publication documented the rise of AI-cloned voice scams, noting that unlike older impersonation attempts, modern AI clones are high-fidelity and specifically targeted.

The Federal Communications Commission warns that grandparent scams have grown increasingly sophisticated, with AI voice cloning adding a dimension of realism that makes verification through voice recognition alone insufficient.

### Protection Measures

- Establish a **family code word** known only to family members, to be used in any emergency call
- **Never send money** based on a phone call without independently verifying the situation
- Call the grandchild directly at their known phone number
- Contact other family members to verify the claimed emergency
- Remember that legitimate law enforcement will never demand bail payment via gift cards, wire transfers, or cryptocurrency

## 7. Summer Travel Scams (June–August)

### Seasonal Trigger Profile

Summer travel season creates fraud opportunities through vacation rental bookings, airline ticket purchases, travel deal advertising, and the general disruption of routine that travel involves. Seniors planning retirement travel are particularly targeted through platforms advertising luxury accommodations and guided tour packages.

### Scale and Impact

Travel fraud surged 18% during summer 2024 compared to the prior year. The BBB and FTC have documented persistent increases in fake vacation rental listings, particularly on platforms where individual property listings are difficult to verify.

### Primary Attack Vectors

**Fake Vacation Rental Listings**
- AI-generated property photos and descriptions posted on legitimate rental platforms
- Listings for properties that don't exist or aren't available for rent
- Requests for payment via wire transfer or cryptocurrency outside the booking platform's payment system
- "Too good to be true" pricing for premium locations during peak season

**AI-Generated Travel Deal Websites**
- Synthetic travel agency websites offering discounted packages
- Fake airline booking sites that harvest credit card information
- Counterfeit hotel reservation confirmation pages

**TSA/Airline Impersonation**
- Phishing messages claiming TSA PreCheck renewal is required (TSA PreCheck renewal is handled only through the official TSA website or enrollment centers)
- Fake flight cancellation notifications requesting rebooking fees
- Fraudulent "travel security clearance" requirements

**Senior-Specific Travel Fraud Patterns**
- Targeting of seniors planning bucket-list or retirement travel with high-value package deals
- Timeshare resale scams (offering to "buy" an existing timeshare for an upfront fee)
- Fake travel insurance products that provide no actual coverage
- "Stranded traveler" scams targeting seniors traveling alone

### AI Enhancement Vectors

- AI-generated property photos that are indistinguishable from real listings
- Chatbot-powered "customer service" on fraudulent booking sites
- AI-generated reviews creating false credibility for non-existent properties
- Synthetic travel agent personas maintaining extended email correspondence

## 9. Scam Trend Data by Quarter

### Q1 (January–March): Tax and Romance Convergence

**Key Metrics:**
- FTC 2024 total reported fraud losses: $12.5 billion (25% increase YoY)
- Romance scam losses estimated at $2.4 billion in 2024 (FTC)
- McAfee survey: 45% of adults 65–74 reported fake tax refund scams
- IRS identity theft case resolution average: 506 days

**AI-Specific Trends:**
- 87% of McAfee survey respondents worried AI is making scams harder to detect
- AI-generated phishing emails now exhibit near-zero grammatical errors, eliminating a traditional detection heuristic
- Voice cloning tools reduced from enterprise-grade to consumer-accessible in 2024–2025

### Q2 (April–June): Transitional Period with Disaster Onset

**Key Metrics:**
- Post-tax-season identity theft claims spike in Q2 as fraudulent returns are detected
- Hurricane season preparedness triggers early disaster relief scam infrastructure deployment
- Travel fraud begins seasonal increase (18% surge documented in summer 2024)

**AI-Specific Trends:**
- AI-generated fake disaster relief websites can be deployed within hours of an event
- Travel booking fraud increasingly uses AI-generated property photos and reviews

### Q3 (July–September): Grandparent Scams and Disaster Fraud Peak

**Key Metrics:**
- IC3 2024: 147,127 elder fraud complaints totaling $4.885 billion
- Canadian grandparent scam ring: $21 million from victims in 46 states (DOJ, March 2025)
- AI voice cloning scams: estimated $5 billion in senior losses in 2024
- Hurricane Helene/Milton (Oct 2024): FEMA fraud indictments in Western District of NC

**AI-Specific Trends:**
- Voice cloning requires only 30 seconds of source audio
- Industrial-scale call centers using AI to manage multi-victim operations simultaneously
- Cryptocurrency laundering of grandparent scam proceeds documented in federal indictments

### Q4 (October–December): Medicare, Holiday, and Year-End Convergence

**Key Metrics:**
- FTC: 45,000+ Medicare-related fraud complaints during 2024 Open Enrollment, losses exceeding $100 million
- FTC December 2025 report: Elder fraud losses reached $2.4 billion (reported) to $81.5 billion (estimated actual) in 2024
- Losses of $100,000+ per incident: 68% of total reported elder fraud dollars
- 300% increase in elder reported losses from 2020 ($600M) to 2024 ($2.4B)

**AI-Specific Trends:**
- Deepfake celebrity endorsements of Medicare plans appearing in social media advertising
- AI-generated fake charity websites proliferating during holiday giving season
- Gift card fraud enhanced by AI-powered social engineering scripts
- Package delivery smishing campaigns using AI-generated tracking interfaces

### Emerging AI-Enabled Seasonal Patterns

1. **Compression of scam deployment timelines**: AI enables near-instantaneous generation of scam infrastructure (websites, phone scripts, phishing materials) in response to seasonal triggers, reducing the lag between event and exploitation
2. **Multi-channel synchronization**: AI coordinates simultaneous phone, email, text, and social media campaigns during peak seasonal windows
3. **Adaptive personalization**: AI tools analyze victim responses in real time and adjust emotional manipulation tactics, increasing conversion rates during vulnerable seasonal periods
4. **Industrialization of romance-to-investment pipeline**: AI chatbots enable single operators to maintain dozens of simultaneous romance scam relationships, with seasonal peaks around Valentine's Day and holidays
5. **Synthetic identity ecosystems**: AI generates complete fake identities — photos, social media histories, voice profiles — that are deployed seasonally (e.g., "Medicare agent" personas in Q4, "IRS agent" personas in Q1)
