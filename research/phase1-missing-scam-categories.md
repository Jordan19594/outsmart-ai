## 1. Tech Support Scams

### How the Scam Works

Tech support scams represent one of the most persistent and financially devastating fraud categories targeting older Americans. The attack typically begins with a fabricated on-screen security alert---a browser pop-up designed to mimic legitimate warnings from Microsoft, Apple, or antivirus vendors---displaying alarming messages such as "YOUR COMPUTER HAS BEEN COMPROMISED" alongside a toll-free number to call for "immediate assistance." Modern variants leverage AI-generated voice systems to staff the inbound call centers, producing convincing American-accented agents who walk victims through granting remote desktop access via tools like AnyDesk, TeamViewer, or ConnectWise.

Once remote access is established, the scammer performs a choreographed routine: opening system logs to display normal error entries as "evidence" of compromise, running fake diagnostic scans, and ultimately steering the victim toward payment. The AI enhancement is threefold. First, AI-generated pop-ups now dynamically adapt to the victim's browser and operating system, rendering them nearly indistinguishable from genuine system alerts. Second, AI chatbots handle initial triage conversations with multiple victims simultaneously before handing off to human operators for the financial extraction phase. Third, deepfake voice technology allows a single call center to present multiple convincing "specialist" personas---a "Level 1 technician," a "senior security analyst," and a "billing department supervisor"---all operated by the same individual.

The financial extraction follows several pathways: direct wire transfers, cryptocurrency deposits via Bitcoin ATMs, gift card purchases (with victims reading card numbers aloud while the scammer records), or---in the most sophisticated variants---the scammer silently initiates transfers while controlling the victim's screen, sometimes accessing bank accounts, brokerage accounts, and even 401(k) retirement plans.

### Recent U.S. Cases and Data (2024--2026)

The scale of tech support fraud targeting seniors has reached extraordinary levels. According to the FTC's December 2025 report to Congress, older consumers reported $159 million in losses to tech support scams in 2024 alone. The FBI's 2024 Internet Crime Report (IC3) documented that individuals aged 60 and older accounted for the largest share of tech support fraud complaints, with total cybercrime losses for this age group reaching $4.8 billion across all categories.

The FTC's August 2025 Data Spotlight revealed that 15% of older adults who lost $10,000 or more to imposter scams (including tech support variants) indicated the scam started with an online ad or pop-up security alert. These pop-up-initiated scams typically described fake alerts impersonating Microsoft or Apple with a phone number to call. In 2024, the number of reports from older adults who lost $10,000 or more to business and government imposter scams---a category encompassing tech support fraud---increased more than fourfold compared to 2020, rising from 1,790 reports to 8,269. Reports of losses exceeding $100,000 increased nearly sevenfold during the same period, with combined reported losses going up eightfold.

Payment methods reveal the sophistication of these operations: 33% of older adults losing $10,000+ to imposter scams paid via cryptocurrency (primarily Bitcoin ATMs), 20% via bank transfer, and 16% in cash. In approximately 21% of cases where losses exceeded $100,000, gold was the payment method---victims were instructed to purchase gold bars and hand them to couriers dispatched by the scammers.

### Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted

Seniors represent the ideal target demographic for tech support scams due to a convergence of factors. Lower digital literacy means older adults are more likely to believe fabricated system alerts are genuine. Many seniors grew up in an era when calling customer support was the standard problem-resolution method, making a phone-based response to a pop-up alert feel natural. Seniors are also more likely to own desktop computers with outdated security software, creating genuine anxiety about vulnerabilities that scammers exploit. Additionally, retirees tend to have significant liquid assets in bank and retirement accounts that can be accessed during a single remote-access session. The isolation factor is critical: the FTC notes that keeping victims on the phone is designed to prevent them from consulting friends or family members who might recognize the fraud.

### Detection Indicators

| Red Flag Category | Specific Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Visual Cues** | Full-screen browser pop-up with flashing warnings; fake Microsoft/Apple logos; a phone number displayed prominently; inability to close the pop-up normally |
| **Verbal Cues** | Caller claims your computer is "sending error signals"; requests to download remote access software; asks you to open Event Viewer to show "proof" of hacking |
| **Financial Cues** | Request for payment via gift cards, Bitcoin ATM, wire transfer, or cash; instructions to move money to "protect" it; requests to access your bank account while screen-sharing |
| **Behavioral Cues** | Urgency and pressure not to hang up; instructions not to tell anyone; caller transfers you to a "supervisor" or "government agent"; asks you to stay on the line while visiting a store |
| **Technical Cues** | Requests to install AnyDesk, TeamViewer, or similar remote access tools; asks you to type commands into your computer; modifies your browser or system settings during the session |

## 2. Medicare/Health Insurance Scams

### How the Scam Works

Medicare and health insurance scams exploit the structural complexity of the U.S. healthcare system---a system that even well-informed beneficiaries find confusing---to steal personal information, commit identity theft, and bill fraudulent charges to government programs. These scams intensify dramatically during Medicare Open Enrollment (October 15--December 7), when legitimate plan changes create a window of plausible outreach.

AI has transformed these scams along multiple vectors. AI-powered voice agents now place robocalls impersonating Medicare representatives, using natural-sounding speech synthesis to inform beneficiaries of "new benefits," "required plan updates," or "expiring coverage." These systems can handle objections, answer basic Medicare questions accurately, and seamlessly transition to extracting the victim's Medicare ID number, Social Security number, and banking information. AI-generated phishing emails and text messages replicate the visual branding of Medicare.gov, the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS), and major insurance carriers with pixel-perfect accuracy.

The fraud operates on multiple levels. At the individual level, scammers use stolen Medicare IDs to submit fraudulent claims for services never rendered---a scheme that costs the U.S. healthcare system an estimated $60 billion annually according to industry estimates. At the identity theft level, the combination of Medicare ID, Social Security number, and date of birth obtained during these calls enables comprehensive financial identity takeover. In the most sophisticated variant, scammers pose as AI-powered "Medicare advisors" offering to help seniors navigate plan selection, building trust through seemingly helpful interactions before extracting financial data.

Dark web medical data markets have added a new dimension: scammers now purchase stolen medical records to craft hyper-personalized cons, referencing a victim's actual prescriptions, physicians, or recent procedures to establish credibility.

### Recent U.S. Cases and Data (2024--2026)

The Better Business Bureau reported a 40% surge in Medicare-related fraud complaints during the 2026 open enrollment period compared to the prior year. The FTC issued a specific consumer alert in September 2025 warning about increased scammer activity during open enrollment season. The FCC has maintained ongoing warnings about Medicare call scams, noting that scammers frequently spoof caller ID numbers to display "Medicare" or "Social Security Administration" on the victim's phone.

Medicare fraud overall remains one of the most costly categories of financial crime in the United States. The National Health Care Anti-Fraud Association estimates that healthcare fraud costs the U.S. tens of billions annually, with seniors bearing the brunt as both direct victims (identity theft) and indirect victims (increased premiums and reduced benefits resulting from systemic fraud). The Senior Medicare Patrol (SMP) program, funded by the Administration for Community Living, has documented increasing use of AI tools in Medicare scam calls, including voice-cloning technology that replicates the voices of victims' actual physicians or insurance agents.

### Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted

Medicare scams target seniors by definition---Medicare eligibility begins at age 65, making every victim a senior. The complexity of Medicare (Parts A, B, C, D, Medigap, and Medicare Advantage) creates genuine confusion that scammers exploit. Many seniors make coverage decisions without professional guidance and welcome what appears to be helpful advice. Open enrollment creates annual urgency and decision pressure. Seniors are also conditioned to receive legitimate outreach from Medicare and insurance companies during this period, making fraudulent contacts harder to distinguish from real ones. The high value of medical identity data---a stolen Medicare ID sells for more on the dark web than a stolen credit card number---makes this population particularly profitable to target.

### Detection Indicators

| Red Flag Category | Specific Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Contact Method** | Unsolicited phone call, text, or email claiming to be from Medicare; caller ID showing "Medicare" or "SSA" (easily spoofed); door-to-door visits from supposed Medicare representatives |
| **Information Requests** | Asks for your Medicare Beneficiary Identifier (MBI); requests Social Security number, bank account, or credit card information; asks you to "verify" your identity with personal details |
| **Offer Characteristics** | Claims of "new Medicare card" requiring your information; offers of free medical equipment, genetic testing, or back braces; promises of special benefits not available through official channels |
| **Pressure Tactics** | Warns coverage will lapse without immediate action; claims you will lose benefits if you do not respond; states enrollment deadline is sooner than the actual date |
| **Verification Failures** | Cannot provide a callback number that matches Medicare's official line (1-800-MEDICARE); refuses to let you verify independently; discourages you from calling your current plan |

## 3. Social Security Administration Impersonation Scams

### How the Scam Works

Social Security Administration (SSA) impersonation scams have evolved into a multi-layered operation that exploits seniors' deep reliance on---and anxiety about---their Social Security benefits. The scam typically begins with a phone call, often robocalled using AI-generated voice technology, informing the victim that their Social Security number has been "suspended," "compromised," or "linked to criminal activity" such as drug trafficking or money laundering. The caller ID is spoofed to display the SSA's actual phone number or a Washington, D.C. area code.

The AI enhancement in these scams is particularly sophisticated. Modern SSA impersonation calls use text-to-speech systems that replicate the cadence and tone of government bureaucracy---measured, authoritative, and procedural. The scammer provides a fake "badge number" and "case number," and may reference the victim's actual last four digits of their Social Security number (obtained from prior data breaches). In the most advanced versions, an AI chatbot handles the initial scripted portion of the call---verifying the victim's name, confirming their "case," and establishing urgency---before transferring to a human operator for the financial extraction phase.

The financial demand follows a consistent pattern: the victim is told they must "protect" their assets by transferring funds to a "secure government account," purchasing gift cards and reading the codes to the agent, depositing cash into a Bitcoin ATM, or withdrawing large amounts of cash for pickup by a "federal courier." The scammer may impersonate multiple agencies in sequence---SSA, then the FTC, then the FBI---creating a tag-team effect that reinforces the illusion of a genuine multi-agency investigation.

### Recent U.S. Cases and Data (2024--2026)

The SSA Office of Inspector General (OIG) confirmed in February 2025 that SSA remains the top government agency impersonated by scammers. As of November 2024, government imposter scam losses for 2024 exceeded $577 million, according to SSA OIG data. The FTC reported that consumers lost more than $12.5 billion to fraud overall in 2024, a 25% increase over the prior year, with imposter scams---led by SSA impersonation---among the largest categories.

In November 2025, CNBC reported on a new variant of Social Security scams in which callers claim the victim's Social Security account has been "flagged for unusual activity" and demand immediate verification of banking information. The FTC's August 2025 Data Spotlight documented that the number of older adults reporting losses of $10,000 or more to government imposter scams increased from 1,790 in 2020 to 8,269 in 2024---a more than fourfold increase. Losses exceeding $100,000 increased nearly sevenfold during the same period.

The FTC noted that 41% of older adults who reported losing $10,000 or more to government or business imposter scams indicated a phone call was the initial contact method. Scammers frequently impersonate real SSA and FTC staff members by name, adding a layer of verifiability that makes the fraud harder to detect.

### Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted

Social Security benefits represent the primary or sole income source for approximately 40% of Americans aged 65 and older. Any threat to these benefits triggers immediate, visceral anxiety that overrides rational deliberation. Seniors interact with SSA more frequently than younger adults (for benefit adjustments, Medicare enrollment, survivor benefits), making contact from the agency feel routine. The authoritarian framing---references to federal law enforcement, arrest warrants, and criminal investigations---exploits the generational tendency among older Americans to comply with perceived government authority. Furthermore, seniors who have experienced legitimate SSA interactions know the agency does make outbound calls in certain circumstances, blurring the line between genuine and fraudulent contacts.

### Detection Indicators

| Red Flag Category | Specific Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Call Characteristics** | Unsolicited call claiming to be from SSA; caller provides a "badge number" or "case number"; robotic-sounding initial greeting that transitions to a live person; caller ID shows SSA's number (1-800-772-1213) or a D.C. area code |
| **Threat Language** | Claims your SSN has been "suspended" (SSA never suspends SSNs); threatens arrest, legal action, or benefit termination; references criminal activity linked to your SSN; warns of "warrants" in your name |
| **Financial Demands** | Requests payment via gift cards, Bitcoin ATM, wire transfer, or cash; instructs you to move money to a "safe" or "government" account; asks for bank account or routing numbers to "verify" your identity |
| **Procedural Red Flags** | Tells you not to contact anyone else about the call; transfers you to another "agency" (FBI, FTC, DEA); asks you to stay on the line while completing financial transactions; requests you confirm personal information they should already have |
| **Verification Failures** | The real SSA will never threaten arrest for non-payment; SSA does not accept payment via gift cards or cryptocurrency; SSA will not call to demand immediate payment; legitimate SSA correspondence comes via mail |

## 4. IRS/Tax Season Scams

### How the Scam Works

IRS impersonation scams follow a seasonal pattern that peaks during tax filing season (January--April) but operates year-round through audit and collections-themed variants. The core mechanism involves scammers contacting victims---by phone, email, text, or increasingly via AI-generated voicemail---claiming to represent the Internal Revenue Service. The victim is told they owe back taxes, face an audit, have a warrant for tax evasion, or are entitled to a refund that requires "verification" of banking details.

AI has amplified these scams in several ways. AI-powered robocall systems can place millions of calls per day using synthesized voices that replicate the bureaucratic tone of government agencies. AI text generators produce phishing emails and letters that closely mimic official IRS correspondence, including accurate formatting, taxpayer identification numbers pulled from breached databases, and references to real tax code provisions. In 2024, AI-powered tax filing tools created a new attack surface: scammers offer fraudulent AI tax preparation services that harvest Social Security numbers, income data, W-2 information, and bank account details submitted by victims seeking automated filing assistance.

The identity theft dimension is particularly severe. In 2024, the IRS received nearly 300,000 reports of identity theft, which the agency estimates led to $5.5 billion in tax fraud. The IRS flagged $16.5 billion in refunds for possible identity fraud in 2024. Stolen senior identities are used to file fraudulent returns and redirect refunds to scammer-controlled accounts---often before the actual taxpayer has filed.

### Recent U.S. Cases and Data (2024--2026)

The IRS flagged $16.5 billion in refunds for possible identity fraud in 2024, according to LifeLock reporting. The IRS received nearly 300,000 reports of identity theft in 2024, leading to an estimated $5.5 billion in tax fraud. Total U.S. cybercrime losses reached $16.6 billion in 2024 (FBI IC3), a 33% increase, with tax-related scams representing a significant component.

J.P. Morgan's 2025 fraud analysis documented the rise of AI-enhanced impersonation attacks across financial services, noting that tax season scams increasingly use deepfake audio and AI-generated correspondence. The FTC's 2024-2025 report on protecting older consumers identified IRS impersonation as a persistent top-tier threat, with older adults disproportionately losing larger amounts per incident compared to younger victims.

### Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted

Seniors' tax situations are often more complex (Social Security benefits, Required Minimum Distributions, investment income, estate considerations), creating genuine anxiety about compliance errors that scammers exploit. Many seniors rely on professional tax preparers and are accustomed to receiving communications about their tax obligations from third parties, making fraudulent contacts feel less unusual. Retirees often have significant assets that make the threat of IRS seizure particularly terrifying. The generational respect for governmental authority---and fear of the IRS specifically---makes seniors more likely to comply with demands rather than challenge the caller's legitimacy. Additionally, seniors who are less digitally fluent are more vulnerable to phishing emails that mimic IRS correspondence, as they may not know how to verify email sender addresses or identify fraudulent URLs.

### Detection Indicators

| Red Flag Category | Specific Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Contact Method** | The IRS initiates contact by mail, not by phone, email, or text; unsolicited calls demanding immediate payment are fraudulent; emails from addresses not ending in .gov |
| **Payment Demands** | Requests for payment via gift cards, wire transfer, or cryptocurrency; demands for immediate payment without opportunity to question or appeal; threats of arrest or deportation for non-payment |
| **Information Requests** | Asks for Social Security number, bank account numbers, or credit card information by phone; requests login credentials for tax filing software; asks you to "verify" your identity using personal data |
| **Offer Red Flags** | Unsolicited claims of a tax refund requiring banking information; offers of free AI tax filing that requests extensive personal data; claims of special tax credits or benefits requiring immediate action |
| **Procedural Verification** | The IRS assigns cases via official correspondence (CP2000, etc.); taxpayers always have the right to question and appeal; the IRS does not threaten law enforcement action over the phone; legitimate IRS notices include a case number verifiable at irs.gov |

## 5. P2P Payment Fraud (Zelle, Venmo, CashApp)

### How the Scam Works

Peer-to-peer (P2P) payment platforms---Zelle, Venmo, and CashApp---have become a primary vector for financial fraud targeting seniors because of a fundamental design characteristic: transactions are instantaneous and largely irreversible. Unlike credit card charges, which can be disputed and reversed, P2P payments function like digital cash. Once sent, funds are typically unrecoverable, and the platforms' terms of service place responsibility on the sender for "authorized" transactions---even those induced by fraud.

The scam operates through several established playbooks. In the impersonation variant, scammers call or text victims posing as their bank's fraud department, warning of "suspicious activity" on their Zelle account. The victim is instructed to "secure" their account by sending a Zelle payment to themselves at a new number---which is actually the scammer's account. In the overpayment variant, a scammer "accidentally" sends a payment and requests a refund; the original payment is then reversed (having been funded by a stolen account), leaving the victim out the refunded amount. In the marketplace variant, scammers use Zelle or Venmo for purchases on platforms like Facebook Marketplace, sending fake payment confirmation screenshots before the money actually arrives.

AI enhancement has made these scams more effective through AI-generated bank alert messages that perfectly replicate the formatting and language of legitimate fraud alerts from major banks, AI chatbots that handle initial victim interactions via text to increase operational scale, and voice-cloning technology that replicates the victim's actual bank representative when escalating to phone calls.

### Recent U.S. Cases and Data (2024--2026)

The U.S. Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations released a damning report finding that the three largest banks on the Zelle network reimbursed scam victims only 12% of the time in 2023. Senator Richard Blumenthal's investigation found that these banks processed billions in Zelle transactions while providing minimal fraud protection. The FTC reported that people reported $470 million in losses to text-based scams in 2024, with P2P payment fraud representing a significant portion. Forbes reported that the FTC received 48,835 reports of payment app or service fraud in 2023 (the most recent complete year available at time of reporting).

BioCatch analysis estimated that up to 0.1% of Zelle transactions involve fraud or scams, translating to approximately 2.8 million fraudulent or scam-related Zelle transactions annually. Zelle processed approximately $1 trillion in payments in 2024, making even a small fraud percentage represent billions in losses. The Senate Banking Committee held two hearings in 2024 specifically addressing Zelle fraud and the banks' failure to protect consumers---particularly seniors---from authorized-payment scams.

### Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted

Seniors adopted P2P payment platforms later than younger demographics and often lack intuitive understanding of how these platforms differ from traditional banking. Many seniors assume that Zelle, because it is integrated into their bank's app, carries the same fraud protections as their bank account---a dangerous misconception. The irreversibility of transactions is counterintuitive to seniors accustomed to the consumer protections of credit cards and checks. Seniors are more likely to trust caller-ID displays and phone-based instructions from someone claiming to be their bank, making the impersonation variant particularly effective. The emotional manipulation---fear that their accounts are compromised---triggers a stress response that impairs the deliberative thinking needed to recognize the scam.

### Detection Indicators

| Red Flag Category | Specific Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Bank Impersonation** | Your "bank" calls about suspicious Zelle activity and asks you to send money to "yourself" at a different number; a text alert about unauthorized transactions includes a number to call that does not match your bank's official line |
| **Payment Requests** | A stranger asks to be paid via Zelle, Venmo, or CashApp for goods or services; someone claims to have "accidentally" sent you money and requests a refund; a buyer insists on paying via P2P rather than in person with cash |
| **Urgency Markers** | Claims your account will be locked or frozen without immediate action; instructions to complete the transaction while staying on the phone; warnings not to visit your bank branch in person |
| **Platform Red Flags** | Requests to send money to a phone number or email you do not recognize; payment confirmations via screenshot rather than in-app notification; requests to use a P2P app you have never used before |
| **Recovery Scams** | After losing money, someone contacts you offering to help recover funds---for a fee; supposed "Zelle support" reaches out via social media; offers to reverse transactions through unofficial channels |

## 6. Gift Card Payment Schemes

### How the Scam Works

Gift card payment schemes represent the extraction endpoint for a wide variety of upstream scams---tech support fraud, government impersonation, romance scams, and grandparent scams all frequently culminate in a demand to purchase gift cards and read the redemption codes to the scammer. The gift card functions as a quasi-anonymous, instantly liquid, and irreversible payment method---once the code is read aloud or photographed, the value is transferred within seconds to the scammer's account and is virtually unrecoverable.

The mechanics are straightforward but psychologically sophisticated. The victim is instructed to visit a retail store (Target, Walmart, Best Buy, CVS), purchase high-denomination gift cards (Apple, Google Play, Amazon, Steam, or prepaid Visa/Mastercard), and immediately call back to read the card numbers and PINs. Scammers frequently instruct victims to purchase cards at multiple stores to avoid triggering retailer fraud-prevention systems that flag large single purchases. Victims are told to remain on the phone while shopping, preventing them from seeking help from store employees or family members. The cover story varies---"paying a fine," "securing your account," "helping a family member in legal trouble"---but the extraction method is consistent.

AI has enhanced gift card scams primarily through scale and personalization. AI-generated robocalls can reach millions of potential victims with convincing government or corporate impersonation. AI chatbots maintain parallel text-based conversations with multiple victims, guiding them through the purchase process in real time. AI-generated deepfake voices are used in grandparent scam variants, where the caller mimics a grandchild's voice to extract emergency gift card payments.

### Recent U.S. Cases and Data (2024--2026)

The FTC's data show that gift cards were the payment method in 16% of fraud loss reports filed by older adults in 2024, making them the second most common payment method after credit cards (26%). The FTC has historically reported that consumers lose more than $200 million annually to gift card scams, with the actual figure likely far higher given underreporting. The FTC's December 2025 report to Congress noted that total fraud losses reported by older adults quadrupled from approximately $600 million in 2020 to $2.4 billion in 2024, with gift cards remaining a primary payment vector throughout this period.

The FTC's gift card data spotlight has consistently shown that about one in four people who report losing money to fraud pay with gift cards. Apple and Google Play cards remain the most commonly demanded brands, followed by Amazon and retail-specific cards. Retailers including Target, Walmart, and Best Buy have implemented point-of-sale warnings and employee training programs, but scammers counter these measures by instructing victims on how to deflect questions from cashiers.

### Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted

Seniors are disproportionately targeted for gift card payment extraction because they are more likely to comply with authority-figure demands without questioning the payment method. Many seniors are unfamiliar with the concept that gift cards can be used as untraceable payment instruments. The physical act of going to a store and purchasing a tangible product feels less risky than a wire transfer or cryptocurrency transaction, reducing the victim's psychological alarm. Seniors are less likely to be aware of retailer fraud-prevention programs and may not recognize that a cashier's questions about the purchase are designed to protect them. The cognitive load of managing an urgent, emotional phone call while navigating a retail store impairs the critical thinking needed to recognize the scam.

### Detection Indicators

| Red Flag Category | Specific Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Payment Demand** | Any legitimate entity asking for payment via gift cards---the IRS, SSA, Medicare, law enforcement, utility companies, and courts never accept gift cards as payment |
| **Purchase Instructions** | Specific instructions on which cards to buy and where; instructions to purchase at multiple stores; demands for high-denomination cards ($200, $500) |
| **Behavioral Control** | Instructions to stay on the phone while purchasing cards; coaching on what to tell the cashier; warnings not to let store employees see the cards or ask questions |
| **Code Extraction** | Request to read the card number and PIN immediately after purchase; instructions to photograph the back of the card and text the image; request to scratch off the PIN coating while on the phone |
| **Cover Stories** | Claims that gift cards are needed to "pay a fine," "protect your account," "help a family member," or "verify your identity"; any scenario where gift cards are presented as an official payment method |

## 7. Package Delivery Scams (USPS/FedEx/Amazon Smishing)

### How the Scam Works

Package delivery scams---a form of "smishing" (SMS phishing)---exploit the ubiquity of online shopping and package tracking to lure victims into clicking malicious links via text messages. The victim receives an unsolicited text message purporting to be from USPS, FedEx, UPS, or Amazon, stating that a package delivery has failed, a shipment requires address confirmation, a customs fee must be paid, or a package is being held and requires action.

The link in the text directs the victim to a meticulously crafted phishing site that replicates the branding of the legitimate carrier. These sites request the victim's personal information (name, address, phone number), financial information (credit card number to pay a small "redelivery fee"), or login credentials. In more sophisticated variants, the phishing site installs malware on the victim's device that harvests stored passwords, banking credentials, and other sensitive data.

AI has supercharged package delivery smishing in three ways. First, AI-generated text messages now perfectly mimic the formatting, URL structures, and language of legitimate carrier notifications, including accurate tracking number formats. Second, AI-powered phishing site generators can spin up hundreds of convincing replica websites in hours, each with unique domains that evade blocklists. Third, large language models enable scammers to generate localized, grammatically perfect messages in any language, eliminating the spelling and grammar errors that previously served as detection cues.

The scale is staggering. Americans received an estimated 19.2 billion spam texts in April 2024 alone---approximately 63 per person---with package delivery scams representing the single largest category.

### Recent U.S. Cases and Data (2024--2026)

The FTC reported that people lost $470 million to text-based scams in 2024, nearly $100 million more than the prior year. Package delivery scams were the most commonly reported text scam category. The FTC's April 2025 Data Spotlight on "Top text scams of 2024" identified fake delivery notifications as the dominant smishing category, noting that the share of text scam reports indicating financial loss increased from 5% in 2020 to 11% in 2024.

The U.S. Postal Inspection Service (USPIS) issued repeated warnings throughout 2024--2025 about smishing campaigns impersonating USPS, noting that USPS does not send unsolicited text messages about packages. AARP reported in 2025 that Americans are "swamped" with scam delivery texts, with USPS, FedEx, and UPS impersonation texts arriving daily for many consumers. The National Council on Aging (NCOA) reported that in April 2024 alone, Americans received 19.2 billion spam texts---roughly 63 per person---with delivery notification scams among the most prevalent.

### Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted

Seniors are increasingly active online shoppers, particularly since the COVID-19 pandemic accelerated e-commerce adoption among older demographics. Many seniors regularly expect package deliveries and find tracking notification texts plausible. Seniors are less likely to recognize subtle differences between legitimate carrier URLs (e.g., usps.com) and spoofed domains (e.g., usps-deliver.com). The small dollar amounts typically requested ($1.99 "redelivery fee," $3.50 "customs charge") reduce the perceived risk of clicking, but the true objective---credential harvesting and identity theft---carries far greater consequences. Seniors are also less likely to have robust spam-filtering technology on their mobile phones and may not know how to report or block suspicious texts.

### Detection Indicators

| Red Flag Category | Specific Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Message Characteristics** | Unsolicited text about a package you do not recall ordering; generic greeting without your name; shortened URLs (bit.ly, tinyurl) rather than official carrier domains; messages from email addresses rather than short codes |
| **URL Red Flags** | Domain does not exactly match the carrier's official website; URL contains extra words (usps-delivery-update.com); website uses HTTP instead of HTTPS; landing page requests credit card information for a small fee |
| **Content Red Flags** | Claims a package cannot be delivered without immediate action; requests for personal information to "verify" your address; threats that a package will be returned if not claimed within 24 hours |
| **Carrier-Specific Rules** | USPS does not send unsolicited text messages; FedEx and UPS track via their apps and official emails; Amazon notifications come through the Amazon app, not text messages; legitimate carriers do not request payment via text |
| **Device Indicators** | After clicking a link, your phone installs an app or downloads a file; your browser is redirected multiple times; you are asked to enter information on a page that looks slightly different from the carrier's official site |

## 8. Facebook Marketplace & Messenger Scams

### How the Scam Works

Facebook Marketplace and Messenger have become fertile ground for fraud targeting seniors, combining the trust dynamics of a familiar social platform with the anonymity of online transactions. Scams on these platforms fall into several categories, each increasingly enhanced by AI tools.

In the buyer scam variant, a fraudster responds to a senior's Marketplace listing, claims to want to buy the item, and sends a fake payment confirmation (a fabricated Zelle, PayPal, or Venmo screenshot) or overpays and requests a refund of the difference. In the seller scam variant, a fraudster lists items at below-market prices to attract buyers, collects payment, and never delivers the goods---or delivers counterfeit or damaged items. In the romance-lure variant, a scammer uses Marketplace interactions as an entry point for longer-term romance or investment fraud.

Messenger-based scams have escalated dramatically. Scammers create fake accounts impersonating real friends or family members, then message seniors requesting money, gift cards, or personal information. AI-generated profile photos (deepfakes) and AI chatbots enable scammers to maintain convincing conversations across hundreds of simultaneous targets. Meta reported in October 2025 that since the start of that year, its teams had detected and disrupted close to 8 million accounts on Facebook and Instagram associated with criminal scam centers targeting people globally.

A particularly insidious variant involves fake customer service accounts. Scammers create Facebook Pages impersonating customer support for airlines, banks, and other companies, then respond to complaints posted on official brand pages, redirecting victims to DMs or Google Forms under the pretense of processing refunds. Meta reported taking action on over 21,000 Pages and accounts engaged in this practice.

### Recent U.S. Cases and Data (2024--2026)

According to the FBI's 2024 Internet Crime Report, people aged 60 and over in the U.S. lost a combined $4.8 billion to fraud in 2024, with social media platforms---Facebook prominently among them---serving as a primary initial contact vector. Meta disclosed in October 2025 that it had detected and disrupted close to 8 million accounts associated with criminal scam centers across Myanmar, Laos, Cambodia, the United Arab Emirates, and the Philippines. These scam centers target people through messaging, dating apps, social media, crypto, and other apps.

Meta also reported taking action on over 21,000 Pages and accounts pretending to be customer support in an attempt to trick people into sharing their information. The Pew Research Center reported in July 2025 that online scams and internet crimes are "skyrocketing," with a record $16.6 billion in losses reported to the FBI in 2024. Meta joined the National Elder Fraud Coordination Center (NEFCC) in October 2025 alongside AARP, Amazon, Capital One, Google, Microsoft, and Walmart to combat fraud targeting older adults.

### Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted

Facebook has the oldest user demographic of any major social media platform, with a disproportionately high percentage of users aged 60 and older. Many seniors use Facebook as their primary social media platform and trust it as a familiar, community-oriented space. The integration of Marketplace into the main Facebook experience means seniors encounter commercial transactions within a social context that feels safer than dedicated e-commerce sites. Seniors may not understand the difference between Facebook-facilitated transactions (which have some buyer protection) and direct payments via Zelle or cash. The Messenger scam variant is particularly effective against seniors because they are more likely to accept friend requests from unfamiliar accounts and less likely to verify identity through other channels before responding.

### Detection Indicators

| Red Flag Category | Specific Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Marketplace Seller Red Flags** | Prices significantly below market value; newly created seller accounts with no history; sellers who refuse to meet in person or at a police exchange zone; requests for payment via wire transfer, gift cards, or cryptocurrency |
| **Marketplace Buyer Red Flags** | Buyers who offer more than asking price; payment confirmations via screenshot rather than in-app notification; requests to ship items before payment clears; buyers who claim to send a "courier" for pickup |
| **Messenger Red Flags** | Messages from friends asking for money or gift cards (account may be compromised); friend requests from people you are already connected with (duplicate account); messages with links to external sites; requests to move conversations to WhatsApp or Telegram |
| **Fake Customer Support** | Responses to your complaints from accounts that are not the official brand page; requests to share personal or financial information via DM; links to Google Forms or external sites for "refund processing" |
| **Account Verification** | Check the account's creation date and activity history; look for inconsistencies in profile photos (AI-generated faces often have asymmetries); verify customer support accounts through the official company website; do not click links received via Messenger from unfamiliar contacts |

## 9. Charity/Disaster Scams

### How the Scam Works

Charity and disaster scams exploit the generous impulse to help those affected by natural disasters, humanitarian crises, and public emergencies. Following hurricanes, wildfires, earthquakes, and other disasters, scammers rapidly deploy fraudulent donation websites, GoFundMe campaigns, and social media appeals designed to capture payments from well-meaning donors. AI has dramatically lowered the barrier to creating convincing fake charities, enabling scammers to generate professional-looking websites, logos, mission statements, and even fake testimonials in minutes.

The AI enhancement is multifaceted. AI image generators create convincing disaster photography that never occurred. AI text generators produce professional charity websites complete with incorporation documents, board member biographies (with AI-generated headshots), and financial statements. AI chatbots staff live chat features on fake charity sites, answering donor questions in real time. AI voice systems power robocall campaigns soliciting donations in the immediate aftermath of disasters, when emotional urgency is highest.

The scam operates through several channels: phishing emails and texts linking to fake donation pages, social media posts with emotional appeals and fabricated images, spoofed websites mimicking established charities (e.g., "American Red Crossing" instead of "American Red Cross"), door-to-door solicitation by individuals claiming to represent disaster relief organizations, and robocalls requesting credit card donations. In some variants, the scammer does not seek direct payment but instead harvests the financial information entered on fake donation forms for subsequent identity theft.

### Recent U.S. Cases and Data (2024--2026)

The FTC, DOJ, and CFPB jointly issued a warning in October 2024 about scams and price gouging in the wake of hurricanes and other natural disasters, noting that scammers "quickly exploit weather emergencies and take advantage of people trying to recover or donate." Reported charity and disaster scam losses reached an estimated $3 billion in 2024, according to FTC data cited in local news reports. The FTC's ongoing consumer alerts have specifically warned about fake charity sites created after major 2024--2025 hurricane seasons, California wildfire emergencies, and international humanitarian crises.

The FCC has issued repeated advisories about charity robocall scams following storms, urging consumers to donate only to trusted, well-known charities and to verify organizations through sites like Charity Navigator, GuideStar, and the BBB Wise Giving Alliance. AI-generated fake charity websites have become sophisticated enough to include functional "About Us" pages, fabricated IRS 501(c)(3) determination letters, and AI-generated images of supposed disaster relief operations.

### Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted

Seniors are disproportionately generous charitable donors---Americans aged 65 and older contribute more per capita to charitable causes than any other age group. This generosity, combined with heightened empathy for disaster victims (many seniors have experienced natural disasters themselves), makes them prime targets. Seniors are more likely to respond to phone-based solicitations, a medium that charity scammers favor. Many seniors donate to charities regularly and may not scrutinize a new solicitation as carefully as a first-time donor. The emotional urgency created by disaster imagery---particularly AI-generated images designed to maximize emotional impact---can override the critical evaluation that would normally flag an unfamiliar charity. Seniors are also less likely to verify a charity's legitimacy through online databases and may accept a convincing website at face value.

### Detection Indicators

| Red Flag Category | Specific Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Organization Verification** | Charity name is similar to but not identical to a well-known organization; cannot be found on Charity Navigator, GuideStar, or the IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search; website was created very recently (check via WHOIS lookup); no verifiable physical address |
| **Solicitation Red Flags** | High-pressure tactics demanding immediate donations; refusal to provide written information about the charity; requests for donations via wire transfer, gift cards, or cryptocurrency; vague descriptions of how funds will be used |
| **Digital Red Flags** | Donation page lacks HTTPS encryption; website contains stock photos or AI-generated images (look for visual artifacts); email solicitations from generic addresses (gmail.com, yahoo.com) rather than organizational domains; social media accounts with minimal history |
| **Communication Red Flags** | Thank-you emails that do not include tax receipt information; inability to provide an EIN (Employer Identification Number); follow-up solicitations that increase in urgency; claims that 100% of donations go directly to victims (legitimate charities have administrative costs) |
| **Verification Steps** | Verify through give.org, charitynavigator.org, or guidestar.org; search the charity name + "scam" online; donate directly through an established charity's official website, not through links in emails or texts; call the charity's officially listed phone number |

## 10. Obituary-Based Targeting of Surviving Spouses

### How the Scam Works

Obituary-based targeting represents one of the most predatory intersections of AI technology and human vulnerability. Cybercriminals systematically scan online obituaries---which function as data-rich public records---using AI tools to extract intimate personal details and identify recently bereaved surviving spouses for targeted fraud. The wealth of information in a typical obituary (birthplace, career history, education, hobbies, family connections, religious affiliation, and sometimes home address) provides scammers with everything needed to construct convincing impersonation personas.

AI language models process obituary text and supplemental information (news articles, social media profiles, corporate directories, property records) to generate complete character profiles of plausible acquaintances---a college classmate, a former colleague, a golf partner, a fellow congregation member. The scammer then contacts the surviving spouse via email, phone, text, or social media, expressing condolences and establishing a connection through fabricated shared memories that include accurate contextual details drawn from the obituary.

The exploitation follows a systematic methodology. After identification of a financially comfortable family with an elderly survivor, the scammer initiates contact through condolences and shared memories. A trust-building phase follows, during which the fabricated persona shares specific details about the deceased that only a genuine acquaintance would know. Once psychological comfort is established, the scammer introduces financial requests---outstanding debts owed by the deceased, investment opportunities the deceased had discussed, charitable donations the deceased had pledged, or requests for "help" from someone the deceased had promised to assist.

Advanced threat actors deploy multiple fake identities simultaneously, creating networks of seemingly legitimate contacts that validate each other, establishing false credibility through manufactured social proof. Fake invoices from funeral homes, purported outstanding debts of the deceased, and fraudulent estate-related legal notices all leverage obituary information to appear legitimate.

### Recent U.S. Cases and Data (2024--2026)

Blackbird.AI published a detailed analysis in May 2025 documenting how AI-powered obituary scams have emerged as a new frontier in targeted phishing. The Ohio Attorney General's office issued a June 2024 warning about "obituary pirates" who use death notices to target surviving family members with fake invoices, identity theft, and impersonation scams. The AARP has maintained ongoing coverage of obituary scams, noting that bereaved individuals are contacted by scammers posing as creditors, insurance agents, and government officials within days of a death notice being published.

While precise dollar figures for obituary-specific fraud are difficult to isolate (these scams are typically reported under broader categories like impersonation or identity theft), the FBI's elder fraud data showing $4.8 billion in losses for adults 60+ in 2024 includes a significant portion of bereavement-targeted fraud. The Regions Bank fraud education program has identified obituary-based scams as a growing concern, noting their intersection with investment fraud (the costliest scam category at $6.5 billion in losses in 2024 per the FBI).

### Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted

The targeting of surviving spouses is inherently an attack on seniors---the likelihood of losing a spouse increases with age, and the vast majority of surviving spouses are 65 or older. Bereavement creates a perfect storm of vulnerability factors. Research cited by Blackbird.AI shows that grief fundamentally alters cognitive processing, reducing the ability to detect manipulation while simultaneously increasing the desire for social connection. This creates optimal conditions for exploitation.

The generational digital divide compounds this vulnerability. Older populations possess less familiarity with AI capabilities and cannot conceive that scammers have technology capable of generating authentic-sounding conversations with accurate personal references. Wealth targeting is deliberate---scammers review obituaries from high-income areas and prioritize targets with significant financial assets. The emotional state of bereavement, combined with the administrative overwhelm of estate settlement, creates a period of diminished vigilance that can last months---precisely the window scammers exploit.

### Detection Indicators

| Red Flag Category | Specific Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Contact Timing** | Unsolicited contact within days or weeks of a death; individuals claiming to be friends or associates of the deceased whom you have never heard of; expressions of condolence that segue into financial requests |
| **Knowledge Red Flags** | The contact knows details from the obituary but cannot provide specific memories that would not be publicly available; stories that are plausible but unverifiable; inability to identify themselves in photographs or provide contact information for mutual acquaintances |
| **Financial Requests** | Claims of debts owed by the deceased that require immediate payment; investment opportunities the deceased had purportedly discussed; charitable donations the deceased had pledged; fake invoices from funeral services, hospitals, or legal firms |
| **Identity Verification** | Request a video call to verify identity; ask for mutual contacts who can confirm the relationship; verify any claimed debts through the estate attorney; do not pay any claimed debts without written documentation verified by legal counsel |
| **Digital Red Flags** | Email addresses that do not match the claimed identity; social media profiles with recent creation dates; phone numbers from unexpected area codes; communications that feel scripted or unnaturally polished (possible AI generation) |

## 11. Pig Butchering Scams (Full Lifecycle Deep-Dive)

### How the Scam Works

Pig butchering (sha zhu pan, 杀猪盘) is a long-term, relationship-based investment fraud that combines romance scam psychology with cryptocurrency investment fraud. The metaphor---fattening a pig before slaughter---describes the patient, methodical approach that distinguishes these operations from traditional quick-hit scams. The lifecycle unfolds over weeks to months, sometimes exceeding a year, and produces average individual losses of $177,000 to $200,000 or more.

**Phase 1: Contact ("Finding the pig").** Scammers initiate contact through dating apps (Tinder, Bumble, Hinge), social media (Instagram, Facebook, LinkedIn), messaging apps (WhatsApp, Telegram), or "wrong number" text messages that appear accidental. They use stolen photos of attractive individuals and fabricate detailed backstories, often posing as successful entrepreneurs, crypto traders, or overseas professionals. AI enhancement at this stage includes AI-generated profile photos that pass reverse image searches, AI chatbots that handle initial outreach across thousands of simultaneous contacts, and AI-powered targeting that selects victims based on social media profiles indicating wealth, loneliness, or relationship-seeking behavior.

**Phase 2: Grooming ("Fattening the pig").** Unlike traditional scams, the operator invests weeks or months building a genuine-feeling relationship. Daily conversations cover life, family, dreams, and aspirations. Fabricated personal stories and photos create emotional intimacy. The scammer gradually introduces their "successful" investment lifestyle, sharing screenshots of supposed profits and luxury purchases. AI tools generate personalized messages at scale, maintain conversation continuity across shifts (multiple operators may manage a single "persona"), and adapt communication style to mirror the victim's language patterns.

**Phase 3: Investment pitch ("Leading to slaughter").** Once trust is established, the scammer introduces a "can't-miss" cryptocurrency trading platform, forex trading app, or DeFi scheme. The platform is entirely controlled by the scam organization but appears legitimate---with professional UI, real-time price feeds, and customer support. The scammer guides the victim through setting up an account and making an initial small investment. This investment shows impressive, fabricated returns, encouraging larger deposits.

**Phase 4: The slaughter.** When the victim has invested a significant sum---often life savings, retirement funds, or borrowed money---the endgame begins. The fake platform freezes withdrawals, citing "taxes," "fees," or "regulatory holds." Each payment toward these fictitious obligations generates new demands. Eventually, the scammer and platform disappear entirely, leaving the victim with catastrophic financial and emotional devastation.

**The human trafficking dimension.** A deeply disturbing aspect of pig butchering is that many of the scammers themselves are victims---trafficked individuals lured by fake job postings and held in compound operations across Southeast Asia. The United Nations estimates over 200,000 people have been trafficked into scam operations in Myanmar and Cambodia alone. Workers are stripped of passports, forced to work 16+ hour days following detailed scripts, and beaten or tortured for failing to meet quotas.

### Recent U.S. Cases and Data (2024--2026)

The FBI's 2024 IC3 Annual Report documented $6.57 billion in U.S. investment fraud losses, with cryptocurrency-related fraud losses reaching $9.3 billion---a 44% year-over-year increase. Pig butchering operations are the single largest category of cryptocurrency scam revenue, according to Chainalysis's 2025 Crypto Crime Report, with on-chain revenue to these operations exceeding $3.6 billion in 2024 (a 40% increase over 2023). Adults aged 60 and older reported more than $2.8 billion in losses tied to crypto-related scams, making them the most financially impacted age group.

The FBI's Operation Level Up, launched in January 2024, has notified 8,103 victims of cryptocurrency investment fraud, with 77% of those victims unaware they were being scammed at the time of notification. The estimated savings to victims through early intervention reached significant levels. The Global Anti-Scam Organization (GASO) has estimated global annual losses to pig butchering at $75 billion or more, with the University of Texas at Austin corroborating these estimates.

TRM Labs' analysis of the FBI's 2024 report highlighted that individuals aged 60 and older reported more than $2.8 billion in losses tied to crypto-related scams, making them the most financially impacted demographic. Wealthmanagement.com reported that sophisticated AI scams have defrauded seniors of $1 billion since 2024, with voice cloning and personalized deception proving especially effective against older adults.

### Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted

While pig butchering scams target a broad demographic (adults aged 30--49 report the highest absolute losses), seniors face unique vulnerability factors. Recently widowed or divorced seniors seeking new social connections are prime targets for the romance component. Seniors with substantial retirement savings, investment portfolios, and home equity represent high-value targets. Many seniors are "crypto-curious"---interested in cryptocurrency but lacking deep knowledge of legitimate exchanges, making them susceptible to sophisticated-looking fake platforms. The isolation that often accompanies aging amplifies vulnerability to emotional manipulation, and the shame associated with falling for a romance scam reduces reporting rates. Seniors are also less likely to recognize the technical signs of a fraudulent trading platform and may not know how to verify a platform's regulatory status through SEC, FINRA, or CFTC databases.

The emotional devastation is compounded by the dual betrayal of losing both a believed relationship and life savings simultaneously. Victim suicides have been documented, making pig butchering one of the most psychologically destructive scam categories.

### Detection Indicators

| Red Flag Category | Specific Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Initial Contact** | Unsolicited contact from a stranger who quickly becomes emotionally invested; "wrong number" texts that lead to friendly conversations; attractive profiles on dating apps with limited photo variety; conversations that move rapidly from a dating app to WhatsApp or Telegram |
| **Relationship Red Flags** | The person can never video chat or always cancels; excuses for not meeting in person (overseas, busy, COVID precautions); declarations of love or deep connection unusually quickly; the relationship feels "too good to be true" |
| **Investment Red Flags** | Casual, repeated mentions of cryptocurrency or forex trading success; offers to "teach" you their investment method; guidance to a specific trading platform you have never heard of; early investments show immediate, impressive returns; encouragement to invest more and claims the "window is closing" |
| **Platform Red Flags** | The trading platform is not registered with the SEC, FINRA, or CFTC; the platform has no verifiable corporate registration; no independent reviews exist on established financial websites; the platform's website was created recently; withdrawal attempts are blocked by demands for fees or taxes |
| **Recovery Scam Warning** | After losing money, someone contacts you offering fund recovery services---for a fee; supposed "Zelle support" or "crypto recovery specialists" reach out via social media; law enforcement impersonators claim they have recovered your funds |

### Full Lifecycle Timeline

| Phase | Duration | Scammer Activity | Victim Experience |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Contact** | Days 1--7 | Initial outreach via dating app, social media, or text; AI-selected targeting | Receives unexpected but pleasant message; engages in casual conversation |
| **Grooming** | Weeks 2--8 | Daily communication; sharing fabricated personal details; building emotional dependency | Develops genuine feelings; shares personal information; trusts the new connection |
| **Introduction** | Weeks 6--12 | Casually mentions investment success; shows profit screenshots; offers to teach | Becomes curious about investment opportunity; sees the contact's apparent wealth |
| **Small Investment** | Weeks 8--14 | Guides victim to fake platform; assists with initial $500--$2,000 deposit | Makes first investment; sees impressive returns on screen; gains confidence |
| **Escalation** | Weeks 10--20 | Encourages larger deposits; provides "insider tips"; celebrates victim's "gains" | Invests $10,000--$100,000+; may access retirement accounts or take loans |
| **Slaughter** | Weeks 16--24+ | Platform freezes withdrawals; demands "taxes" and "fees"; fabricates regulatory excuses | Attempts to withdraw funds; pays additional fees in hope of recovery |
| **Disappearance** | Final | Scammer and platform go offline; phone numbers disconnected | Discovers total loss; experiences severe financial and emotional devastation |

## Methodology Note

This research was compiled in February 2026 using publicly available data from federal agencies (FTC, FBI IC3, SSA OIG, IRS, FCC), industry reports (Chainalysis, TRM Labs, BioCatch), nonprofit organizations (AARP, NCOA, GASO), technology companies (Meta), academic research, and investigative journalism. Dollar figures represent reported losses, which the FTC estimates represent only a fraction of actual losses---the true cost of fraud targeting seniors is likely several multiples of reported figures. All statistics are from the most recent available reporting period, primarily 2024 data published in 2025.
