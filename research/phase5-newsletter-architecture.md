# Senior Scam Shield: Newsletter Architecture & Editorial Framework

**Document Type:** Editorial Blueprint & Production Guide
**Version:** 1.0 | February 2026
**Audience:** Newsletter production team, designers, content strategists
**Purpose:** Comprehensive framework for producing a recurring PDF newsletter that protects U.S. seniors (65+) from AI-enabled financial scams

---

## 1. Newsletter Identity & Positioning

### Proposed Names

| # | Name | Tagline | Rationale |
|---|------|---------|-----------|
| 1 | **Scam Shield** | *Your Monthly Guide to Staying Safe and Savvy* | Direct, empowering, implies protection without fear |
| 2 | **Wise & Wary** | *Smart Protection for Smart People* | Frames readers as intelligent; "wary" as a positive trait |
| 3 | **Safe & Sound** | *Straight Talk About Today's Scams* | Warm, familiar phrase; positions the newsletter as a trusted friend |
| 4 | **The Alert** | *Clear Answers for a Confusing Digital World* | Connotes awareness without alarm; news-forward positioning |
| 5 | **Guard Your Ground** | *Practical Scam Defense, Delivered Monthly* | Action-oriented; dignity of standing firm rather than being a victim |

**Recommended lead:** "Scam Shield" -- strongest brand recall, clear purpose from the name alone, works as both a noun and a concept.

### Brand Voice Guidelines

| Attribute | Guideline |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Tone** | Warm, confident, neighborly. Like a trusted friend who happens to be an expert. Never condescending, never alarmist. |
| **Vocabulary** | Everyday language. If a 12-year-old would not know the word, replace it or define it on first use. |
| **Sentence length** | Target 12-18 words per sentence. Maximum 25 words. Break longer ideas across two sentences. |
| **Paragraph length** | 2-4 sentences maximum. One idea per paragraph. |
| **Reading level** | Flesch-Kincaid Grade 6-8. Test every article before publication. |
| **Person** | Second person ("you") for instructions. First person plural ("we") for shared experience. Avoid third person ("seniors" or "the elderly"). |
| **Emotional register** | Empowerment over fear. Capability over vulnerability. Knowledge over warning. |

### Competitive Differentiation

| Existing Resource | Gap This Newsletter Fills |
|-------------------|--------------------------|
| **AARP Fraud Watch Network** | AARP covers broad fraud; this newsletter focuses exclusively on AI-enabled scams with step-by-step visual guides |
| **FTC Consumer Alerts** | FTC alerts are reactive and legalistic; this newsletter is proactive, uses storytelling, and provides family conversation tools |
| **FBI IC3 Reports** | IC3 data is statistical and law-enforcement oriented; this newsletter translates data into personal, actionable protection |
| **Local news coverage** | News reports on scams after the fact; this newsletter teaches pattern recognition before scams strike |
| **Bank fraud advisories** | Bank materials are generic; this newsletter addresses AI-specific threats like voice cloning, deepfake video, and AI-written phishing |

**Core differentiator:** The only recurring publication that combines AI-scam specificity, senior-optimized design, family engagement tools, and community distribution in a single monthly package.

## 2. Recurring Segment Framework

### Segment 1: Scam of the Month

| Detail | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Purpose** | Deep breakdown of one trending AI-enabled scam, teaching readers exactly how it works and how to spot it |
| **Format** | Narrative explanation with a "How It Works" numbered sequence, red-flag checklist, and "What To Do" action box |
| **Word count** | 500-700 words |
| **Placement** | Pages 2-3 (lead story after cover) |

**Example (Issue #1):** *AI Voice Cloning -- When "Your Grandchild" Calls for Help*

> Imagine getting a phone call and hearing your grandson's voice, panicked, saying he is in trouble and needs money right away. The voice sounds exactly like him. But it is not him. Scammers are now using artificial intelligence to copy anyone's voice from just a few seconds of audio found on social media. Here is how the scam works, step by step, and how to protect yourself.

How It Works:
1. The scammer finds a short video of your family member on Facebook or Instagram.
2. AI software copies the voice in under 30 seconds.
3. The scammer calls you using a phone number that looks like it is from your family member's area code.
4. The fake voice says they are in jail, in an accident, or stranded -- and begs you not to tell anyone.
5. They ask you to send money by gift card, wire transfer, or cryptocurrency.

Red Flags:
- Caller says "Don't tell Mom and Dad"
- Asks for gift cards or wire transfers
- Creates extreme urgency ("I need it in the next hour")
- Call comes from an unknown number, not their usual phone

What To Do:
- Hang up. Call your family member directly at their real number.
- Establish a family code word that only your family knows.
- Never send money based on a phone call alone.

### Segment 2: Real Story

| Detail | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Purpose** | Anonymized case study of a real scam experience -- builds empathy, reduces shame, teaches through narrative |
| **Format** | First-person or close-third-person storytelling with a "Lessons Learned" sidebar |
| **Word count** | 300-450 words |
| **Placement** | Page 4 |

**Example (Issue #1):** *"I Thought It Was My Bank Calling" -- Margaret's Story*

> Margaret, 72, from Ohio, received a call that appeared to come from her bank's real phone number. The caller knew her name, her account type, and the last four digits of her card. They said someone had made a suspicious purchase and they needed to verify her identity. Margaret followed their instructions -- and lost $4,200 in 20 minutes. "I felt so foolish," she says, "but the caller knew things only my bank should know." Margaret is not foolish. She was targeted by criminals using AI tools that can spoof caller ID and scrape personal details from data breaches. She reported the scam and recovered part of her money. Her advice: "If your bank calls you, hang up and call the number on the back of your card."

**Lessons Learned (sidebar):**
- Banks will never ask you to move money to a "safe" account.
- Caller ID can be faked. The number on your screen may not be real.
- It is always safe to hang up and call back using a number you trust.

**Editorial note:** All Real Story segments must be written with dignity. The subject is never "gullible" or "naive." The scammer is always the one at fault. Include the outcome (reported, recovered money, now helps others) to model positive action.

### Segment 3: Ask the Expert

| Detail | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Purpose** | Q&A format answering real reader questions (or commonly asked questions) about AI scams and digital safety |
| **Format** | 2-3 questions per issue, answered in plain language by a named expert (rotating: cybersecurity pro, law enforcement, consumer advocate) |
| **Word count** | 250-400 words total |
| **Placement** | Page 5 |

**Example (Issue #1):**

**Q: Can scammers really make a fake video of someone I know?**
A: Yes. This is called a "deepfake." AI software can create a video that looks and sounds like a real person. These videos are getting harder to spot. If you see a video of someone asking for money or sharing an unusual request, verify it by contacting that person directly through a method you trust, such as calling their phone number.

**Q: My email said my account was "compromised." Is that real?**
A: Most of these emails are fake. Real companies rarely use urgent or scary language. Look for these clues: misspelled words, a sender address that does not match the company, and links that ask for your password. When in doubt, do not click. Go directly to the company's website by typing the address into your browser.

### Segment 4: Tech Tip of the Month

| Detail | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Purpose** | One single, actionable technology protection step with clear visual instructions |
| **Format** | Step-by-step walkthrough with numbered screenshots or illustrations. Maximum 5 steps. |
| **Word count** | 200-300 words (visuals carry much of the communication) |
| **Placement** | Page 6 |

**Example (Issue #1):** *How to Turn On Two-Step Verification for Your Email*

> Two-step verification adds a second lock to your email account. Even if someone steals your password, they cannot get in without a code sent to your phone. Here is how to set it up for Gmail:

Step 1: Open Gmail on your computer. Click on your profile picture in the top right corner.
Step 2: Click "Manage your Google Account."
Step 3: Click "Security" on the left side of the screen.
Step 4: Under "How you sign in to Google," click "2-Step Verification."
Step 5: Click "Get Started" and follow the instructions. Choose "Text message" to receive codes on your phone.

[Each step accompanied by a screenshot with the relevant button circled in red]

**Why This Matters (callout box):** Over 80% of hacked accounts did not have two-step verification turned on. This one step blocks most attackers, even if they have your password.

### Segment 5: Family Talk

| Detail | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Purpose** | Conversation guide to help families discuss scam risks respectfully, without making the older adult feel patronized |
| **Format** | "Conversation starters" with suggested phrasing, a "Do Say / Don't Say" table, and a tear-out card format |
| **Word count** | 250-350 words |
| **Placement** | Page 7 |

**Example (Issue #1):** *Starting the Conversation About Phone Scams*

**Do Say / Don't Say:**

| Instead of... | Try saying... |
|---------------|---------------|
| "You need to be more careful" | "I read about a new scam that is targeting everyone -- can I share it with you?" |
| "Don't answer calls from numbers you don't know" | "I've started letting unknown calls go to voicemail. It's been helpful -- want to try it together?" |
| "You could get scammed" | "These scammers are so sophisticated that even tech experts get fooled" |
| "Let me set up your phone for you" | "Want to sit together and set up some extra protection on both our phones?" |

**Conversation Starter Card (tear-out):**
"Did you hear about the new scam where criminals can copy someone's voice using AI? I thought we could pick a family code word -- something only we would know -- so if anyone ever calls pretending to be one of us, we can verify it's really them. What word should we pick?"

### Segment 6: Quick Spot Check

| Detail | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Purpose** | Interactive 3-question quiz to practice scam detection skills. Builds confidence through low-stakes practice. |
| **Format** | Multiple choice or "Scam or Legit?" format with answers and explanations on the following page |
| **Word count** | 150-200 words |
| **Placement** | Page 8 (answers on page 9) |

**Example (Issue #1):**

**Question 1:** You receive a text from "your bank" saying: "Suspicious activity detected. Click here to verify your account: bit.ly/xK29mQ." Is this likely real or a scam?
- A) Real -- my bank monitors my account
- B) Scam -- the link looks suspicious
- C) Not sure

**Answer:** B. Banks do not send links through text messages that use shortened URLs. A real bank message would direct you to log in through their official app or website.

**Question 2:** A caller says they are from the IRS and you owe back taxes. They say you will be arrested if you do not pay today by gift card. Is this real or a scam?
- A) Real -- the IRS can arrest people
- B) Scam -- the IRS does not call or demand gift cards

**Answer:** B. The IRS contacts people by mail first, never by phone demanding immediate payment, and never accepts gift cards.

**Question 3:** You see a Facebook ad for a product at 90% off. The website looks professional. You have never heard of the company. Should you buy?
- A) Yes -- the website looks real
- B) No -- research the company first

**Answer:** B. AI can now create professional-looking websites in minutes. Search the company name plus the word "scam" before purchasing.

## 3. Design & Layout Guidelines

### Page Specifications

| Element | Specification |
|---------|---------------|
| **Page count** | 10-12 pages per issue (including cover and back page) |
| **Page size** | U.S. Letter (8.5" x 11") for print compatibility |
| **Margins** | Minimum 0.75" on all sides (1" preferred for print binding) |
| **Columns** | Single column for body text; two-column only for sidebars and resource lists |
| **Orientation** | Portrait |

### Typography

| Element | Font Size | Style Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Body text** | 14pt minimum (16pt preferred) | Serif or highly legible sans-serif (e.g., Georgia, Merriweather, Open Sans). Research from the Lighthouse for the Blind and the American Foundation for the Blind recommends 16-18pt for older readers. |
| **Headers (H1)** | 24-28pt | Bold, high contrast |
| **Headers (H2)** | 20-22pt | Bold or semi-bold |
| **Captions** | 12pt minimum | Italics used sparingly |
| **Callout boxes** | 14-16pt | Bold key phrases |
| **Line spacing** | 1.4-1.6x | Generous leading improves tracking for aging eyes |
| **Letter spacing** | Slightly expanded | Avoid condensed or compressed fonts entirely |
| **Font families to avoid** | Script, decorative, thin-weight, all-caps body text | |

### Color & Contrast

| Guideline | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Contrast ratio (body text)** | Minimum 7:1 (WCAG AAA) -- exceeds the 4.5:1 AA minimum to account for age-related vision changes |
| **Contrast ratio (large text/headers)** | Minimum 4.5:1 |
| **Background** | White or very light cream (#FFFFFF or #FFF8F0) |
| **Body text color** | Near-black (#1A1A1A or #222222) -- pure black on white can cause eye strain |
| **Accent color (links, highlights)** | Deep blue (#1A4B8C) or teal (#006B6B) -- avoid red/green combinations due to color blindness prevalence in older adults |
| **Warning/alert color** | Dark orange (#C94400) with icon reinforcement -- never rely on color alone to convey meaning |
| **No color-only information** | All color-coded information must also have text labels or icons |

### White Space & Layout

- Leave generous margins between sections (minimum 0.5" vertical space between segments)
- Use horizontal rules or subtle background shading to separate sections
- Avoid dense "wall of text" layouts -- break content with callout boxes, pull quotes, and illustrations
- Each page should have at least 25% white space
- Avoid text wrapping around images on more than one side

### Visual Elements

| Element | Guideline |
|---------|-----------|
| **Icons** | Simple, flat-style line icons. Avoid 3D or photorealistic icons. Each icon should be at least 40x40px. Use consistently throughout all issues. |
| **Illustrations** | Warm, inclusive, diverse representation. Avoid stock photos that look staged. Prefer illustration style over photography for consistency. |
| **Callout boxes** | Three types: (1) Warning (orange border + alert icon), (2) Tip (blue border + lightbulb icon), (3) Action Item (green border + checkmark icon) |
| **Checklists** | Use large checkbox squares (at least 14pt) with clear labels. Print-friendly so readers can physically check items off. |
| **QR codes** | Minimum 1" x 1". Always paired with a plain-text URL alternative below. Include a brief label: "Scan with your phone camera or type the web address below." |
| **Screenshots** | Annotated with red circles/arrows. Cropped to show only the relevant portion. Minimum 4" wide. Labeled with device type ("This is what it looks like on an iPhone"). |

### Print-Friendly Requirements

- No bleed-edge color (wastes ink and creates print artifacts)
- All content readable in grayscale
- Page numbers on every page (bottom center, 12pt minimum)
- Table of contents on page 1 or inside cover
- Perforated tear-out cards for Family Talk conversation starters (production consideration)

## 5. Distribution Strategy Considerations

### PDF Format Optimization

| Consideration | Guideline |
|---------------|-----------|
| **File size** | Target under 5 MB per issue. Compress images to 150 DPI (sufficient for screen; acceptable for print). Offer a separate "print quality" version at 300 DPI if needed. |
| **Accessibility** | Tag all PDF elements for screen reader compatibility (headings, reading order, alt text on images). Use PDF/UA standard. |
| **Bookmarks** | Include PDF bookmarks for each segment so readers can navigate by section. |
| **Hyperlinks** | Make all URLs clickable in the digital version. Use full URLs (not "click here") so they are visible when printed. |
| **File naming** | Consistent format: `ScamShield-2026-01-January.pdf` |

### Email Delivery

| Element | Best Practice |
|---------|---------------|
| **Subject lines** | Clear, specific, no hype. Examples: "Your January Scam Shield Is Here -- This Month: Tax Season Traps" or "New Issue: How Scammers Copy Voices with AI" |
| **Subject line length** | 40-60 characters (visible on most mobile and desktop email clients) |
| **Sender name** | Consistent and recognizable: "Scam Shield Newsletter" (not a personal name that may seem unfamiliar) |
| **Send time** | Tuesday or Wednesday, 9:00-10:00 AM local time. Research shows older adults check email in the morning. Avoid weekends and Mondays. |
| **Frequency** | Monthly (first Tuesday of each month). Consistent schedule builds habit and trust. |
| **Email body** | Brief summary (3-4 sentences) of what is in this month's issue plus a large, clearly labeled "Read This Month's Issue" button linking to the PDF. Do not embed the full newsletter in the email body. |
| **Plain text alternative** | Always include a plain-text version of the email for readers whose email clients do not display HTML. |
| **Unsubscribe** | Clear, one-click unsubscribe. Required by law (CAN-SPAM) and builds trust. |

### Print-Friendly Distribution

| Channel | Implementation |
|---------|----------------|
| **Senior centers** | Partner with Area Agencies on Aging (AAA) to distribute printed copies at senior centers. Provide a print-ready PDF with crop marks. |
| **Public libraries** | Coordinate with library systems (reference WebJunction's fraud prevention program for libraries as a model). Place in high-traffic areas near public computers. |
| **Houses of worship** | Offer to church, synagogue, and mosque bulletin coordinators as a monthly insert or lobby handout. |
| **Healthcare offices** | Place in waiting rooms of primary care physicians, audiologists, and ophthalmologists (where seniors have idle time to read). |
| **Pharmacies** | Partner with independent pharmacies for counter display. |
| **Meals on Wheels** | Include printed copy with meal deliveries for homebound seniors. |

### Partnership Opportunities

| Partner Type | Opportunity |
|-------------|-------------|
| **AARP local chapters** | Co-branded distribution at chapter meetings and events. Leverage AARP's Fraud Watch Network for content validation. |
| **Credit unions & community banks** | In-branch distribution, co-branded with the institution's logo. Banks benefit from reduced fraud losses. |
| **State Attorney General offices** | Content partnership for state-specific scam alerts. Many AG offices have senior fraud units. |
| **Adult Protective Services** | Distribution through APS caseworkers to at-risk populations. |
| **ROSE Advocacy / Seniors vs Crime** | Content collaboration and distribution through existing anti-fraud networks for older adults. |
| **Local law enforcement** | Community policing divisions can distribute at senior safety events and neighborhood watch meetings. |

## 6. Tone & Language Style Guide

### Words to Use (Plain Language Alternatives)

Write as if you are explaining something to a smart friend who is not a technology expert.

| Technical Term | Senior-Friendly Alternative |
|---------------|----------------------------|
| Phishing | Fake email (or text) designed to trick you |
| Malware | Harmful software that can damage your computer |
| Ransomware | Software that locks your files and demands payment |
| Two-factor authentication (2FA) | Two-step verification -- a second lock on your account |
| Deepfake | AI-created fake video or audio that looks and sounds real |
| AI (artificial intelligence) | Computer software that can learn and imitate human behavior |
| Voice cloning | Copying someone's voice using AI software |
| Encryption | Scrambling your information so only you can read it |
| VPN | A secure, private connection for your internet (like a tunnel) |
| URL | Web address (the text you type into your browser) |
| Browser | The program you use to go on the internet (Safari, Chrome, Firefox) |
| Spam | Unwanted junk messages |
| Spoofing | Making a fake phone number or email look real |
| Data breach | When a company's customer information is stolen by hackers |
| Cryptocurrency | Digital money (like Bitcoin) that exists only on computers |
| Social engineering | Tricks scammers use to manipulate people into sharing information |
| Dark web | A hidden part of the internet used for illegal activity |
| Firewall | A security barrier that blocks unwanted access to your computer |
| Cloud storage | Saving files on the internet instead of on your computer |
| Biometrics | Using your fingerprint or face to unlock a device |
| Pop-up | A small window that suddenly appears on your screen |
| Cookie (web) | A small file a website saves on your computer to remember you |
| Bandwidth | How much data your internet connection can handle at one time |
| Phishing link | A fake web address designed to steal your information |
| Multi-factor authentication | Using more than one way to prove it is really you when signing in |

### Words and Phrases to Avoid

| Avoid | Why | Use Instead |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| "Elderly" | Perceived as diminishing | "Older adults" or simply "you" |
| "Victim" (as identity) | Reduces the person to the crime | "Person who was targeted" or "person who experienced a scam" |
| "You should have known" | Blaming language | "These scams are designed to fool anyone" |
| "Simple" / "Easy" | Implies failure if the reader finds it difficult | "Here are the steps" / "This takes about 5 minutes" |
| "Cyber" (without context) | Jargon-heavy | Spell out: "online" or "internet-related" |
| Acronyms without definition | Excludes readers | Define on first use in every issue (do not assume readers remember from prior issues) |
| "You must" / "You need to" | Authoritarian tone | "We recommend" / "A good step is to" |
| "Devastating" / "Terrifying" | Fear-based language | "Costly" / "Concerning" / "Something to watch for" |
| "Senior moment" | Demeaning | Never use |
| "Tech-savvy" (implying others are not) | Creates an in-group/out-group | "People of all experience levels" |

### Sentence Structure Guidelines

1. **Lead with the action or benefit.** "To protect your account, turn on two-step verification" (not "Two-step verification, which is a security feature offered by most email providers, can help protect your account").
2. **One idea per sentence.** Break compound sentences into two.
3. **Use active voice.** "The scammer sends a fake email" (not "A fake email is sent by the scammer").
4. **Define terms immediately.** "Phishing -- a type of fake email designed to trick you -- is the most common online scam."
5. **Use concrete examples.** Instead of "scammers use social engineering," write "scammers may pretend to be your bank, your grandchild, or a government official."

### The Analogy-First Approach

When explaining technical concepts, always lead with a familiar comparison:

| Concept | Analogy |
|---------|---------|
| Two-step verification | "Like having two locks on your front door. Even if someone picks one, they still can't get in." |
| Password manager | "Like a secure address book that remembers all your passwords for you." |
| Data breach | "Imagine someone broke into a store and copied all the customer files. That's what happens in a data breach." |
| Voice cloning | "Think of it like a high-tech parrot that can perfectly imitate anyone's voice after hearing just a few words." |
| Spoofing | "It's like someone putting a fake return address on an envelope. The name looks real, but the letter isn't from who you think." |
| Encryption | "Like writing a letter in a secret code that only you and the person you're sending it to can read." |
| Firewall | "Like a security guard at the door of your computer, checking who should be allowed in." |

### Writing About Scam Victims with Dignity

**Core principles:**

1. **The scammer is always the criminal.** Frame every story around the scammer's tactics, not the victim's mistakes. "The scammer used a convincing fake website" not "She fell for a fake website."
2. **Intelligence is not a factor.** State explicitly that scam targets include doctors, lawyers, professors, and tech professionals. Scammers succeed through psychological manipulation, not because their targets lack intelligence.
3. **Use survivor language when appropriate.** "Margaret experienced a scam and took action to recover" rather than "Margaret was victimized."
4. **Always include the positive outcome.** What did the person do after? Reported it, warned others, recovered funds, now volunteers. End on strength.
5. **Never reveal identifying details** without explicit written consent. Change names, locations, and specifics.

### Empowerment vs. Fear Patterns

| Fear-Based (Avoid) | Empowerment-Based (Use) |
|--------------------|------------------------|
| "Scammers are coming for your money" | "Here's how to stay one step ahead" |
| "You could lose everything" | "A few simple steps can protect your finances" |
| "Seniors are the #1 target" | "Knowing what to look for is your strongest defense" |
| "Don't trust anyone" | "Trust, but verify -- here's how" |
| "It's getting worse every day" | "More people are learning to spot and stop scams" |
| "You are vulnerable" | "You are capable of protecting yourself" |

### Theme: "Your First Line of Defense"
### Publication Date: January 2026 (or first issue launch)
